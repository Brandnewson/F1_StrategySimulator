\chapter{Introduction and Background Research}
\label{chapter1}

\section{Introduction}
Formula 1 strategy is a decision problem under pressure. Teams choose when to defend, when to attack, and when to protect tyres while conditions keep changing. A single tactical choice can improve race position or cause time loss that cannot be recovered. This makes the domain suitable for reinforcement learning, where an agent learns from repeated interaction with an environment rather than from a fixed answer key \citep{ribeiro2002rlagents}.

This dissertation investigated competitive race strategy as a multi-agent reinforcement learning problem under uncertainty. The project did not claim to reproduce full real-world Formula 1 operations. Instead, it used an F1-inspired simulator as a controlled testbed for asking how interacting learning agents adapt strategy when the environment is stochastic, opponents are also decision-makers, and race outcomes depend on both local tactical choices and evolving multi-agent interaction.

\subsection{Project context and key definitions}
The field is sequential decision making under uncertainty. The niche is multi-agent strategic decision making in a simplified motorsport simulator where race outcomes emerge from repeated interaction between learning agents.

The phrase \textit{competitive race among AI agents} had a precise meaning in this project. Each car was controlled by its own policy, and those policies learned in an environment where every agent affected the state transitions seen by the others. Agents still influenced one another through shared race dynamics such as overtaking attempts, position changes, contested track position, and evolving time gaps. This created the mixed cooperative-competitive structure that makes MARL both relevant and difficult in stochastic environments.

The phrase \textit{stochastic environment} also had a precise meaning. The same state-action pair could lead to different outcomes because overtake resolution is probabilistic and race context evolves under uncertainty. In a multi-agent setting this uncertainty is amplified by non-stationarity, because each policy is learning against opponents whose behaviour is also changing \citep{busoniu2008survey,hernandezleal2019survey}. This non-determinism was central to the investigation rather than a nuisance variable \citep{heilmeier2018racesim,cappello2025tires}.

\subsection{MARL framing in stochastic races}
From a research perspective, the strategy problem was treated as multi-agent reinforcement learning in a stochastic game setting: policies adapted while opponents also adapted, creating non-stationarity, credit-assignment difficulty, and strategic feedback loops \citep{busoniu2008survey,hernandezleal2019survey}. The study therefore treated MARL not as an extension of a single-agent problem, but as the main object of investigation. The central question was whether interacting race policies could remain robust as stochasticity increased, while still producing interpretable strategic behaviour.

This framing also gave the dissertation a concrete implementation path. The first credible MARL baseline was defined as a race in which all competitors were concurrently learning independent DQN-family policies. This kept the environment genuinely multi-agent while providing a simple and defensible value-based starting point. From there, stronger MARL structure was framed through centralised-training/decentralised-execution methods and value factorisation, particularly the progression from independent learners toward VDN and QMIX-style approaches \citep{foerster2017stabilising,sunehag2018vdn,rashid2018qmix}. Actor-critic MARL remained relevant context through COMA and MADDPG, but not the primary implementation family for this dissertation \citep{foerster2018coma,lowe2017maddpg}. This overall framing aligned with recent motorsport multi-agent work and provided a principled basis for analysing strategy emergence under uncertainty \citep{liu2024formulae,fieni2026learning}.

\subsection{Why DQN-family in this MARL study}
The DQN family was chosen as the core value-based comparator group for five explicit research reasons. First, the tactical strategy actions in this simulator are discrete, which is a natural fit for Q-learning style control \citep{zhu2021actionspaces,mnih2015human}. Second, off-policy learning with replay improves reuse of experience from repeated stochastic scenarios, which is important for sample-efficient simulator studies \citep{mnih2015human,schaul2016prioritized}. Third, stabilisation extensions such as Double and Dueling DQN directly target overestimation and representation issues that can distort learning in noisy competitive settings \citep{vanhasselt2016double,wang2016dueling}. Fourth, Rainbow-style integration provides a strong combined benchmark of DQN improvements for rigorous comparative baselines \citep{hessel2018rainbow}. Fifth, this family supports clear reproducibility and comparability under matched-budget evaluation, which is critical when conclusions must remain statistically defensible \citep{henderson2018deep,agarwal2021statistical}.

This choice did not weaken the MARL identity of the paper. Instead, it made the implementation path coherent: MARL was the research framing, and DQN was the chosen value-based family used to operationalise that investigation. Independent DQN learners formed the first baseline because they preserve genuine non-stationary multi-agent interaction, while stronger CTDE and value-factorisation methods such as VDN and QMIX provide the natural next step when more explicit coordination structure is required \citep{sunehag2018vdn,rashid2018qmix}. PPO and MADDPG therefore remained important reference points for interpretation, but they were not the main implementation family because the study prioritised discrete tactical control, replay efficiency, and matched-family comparability \citep{schulman2017ppo,lowe2017maddpg}.

\subsection{Aim}
This dissertation investigated how MARL policies learned race strategy under stochastic uncertainty, with emphasis on robustness, interaction-driven behaviour, and reliable empirical comparison. Within that MARL focus, the study explicitly justified and evaluated the DQN family as the core value-based comparator group and used independent concurrent DQN learners as the first implementation baseline for a genuinely multi-agent race environment.

\subsection{Objectives}
The objectives were defined to support a MARL-first research investigation while preserving evidential rigor.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Characterised how interacting RL agents adapted strategy under stochastic race dynamics, with robustness as a primary outcome dimension.
    \item Identified and interpreted emergent strategic behaviours arising from mixed cooperative-competitive interaction.
    \item Justified and evaluated DQN-family methods as the principal value-based comparator group for this MARL use-case \citep{mnih2015human,vanhasselt2016double,wang2016dueling,schaul2016prioritized,hessel2018rainbow}.
    \item Enforced statistically reliable comparison practice through matched-budget evaluation, seed control, and uncertainty-aware reporting \citep{henderson2018deep,agarwal2021statistical}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research questions and hypotheses}
\textbf{RQ1.} How robustly did MARL policies maintain finish-first performance as stochastic uncertainty increased.

\textbf{H1.} MARL policies with stronger value-stabilisation mechanisms retained higher finish-first performance and lower seed-sensitive variability under increased stochasticity.

\textbf{RQ2.} What strategic behaviours emerged from mixed cooperative-competitive interaction, and how did those behaviours relate to race outcomes.

\textbf{H2.} Better-performing policies exhibited selective overtaking, improved risk calibration, and more stable race management than policies that only maximized local tactical events.

\textbf{RQ3.} Why was the DQN family an appropriate comparator group for MARL strategy learning in this stochastic use-case, and which DQN variants were most reliable under controlled evaluation.

\textbf{H3.} DQN variants with established stabilisation extensions (Double, Dueling, replay prioritisation, Rainbow-style integration) produced more reliable comparative outcomes than a plain value baseline under matched controls \citep{vanhasselt2016double,wang2016dueling,schaul2016prioritized,hessel2018rainbow}.

\section{Literature Review}
\subsection{Review strategy and evidence quality}
This chapter uses a thematic review strategy rather than a chronological catalogue. The search emphasis is on primary technical literature in motorsport strategy modelling, reinforcement learning algorithm design, and reproducible evaluation practice.

The evidence base is separated into three quality tiers. Core evidence includes peer-reviewed motorsport strategy papers and canonical reinforcement learning method papers. Supporting evidence includes recent domain-specific preprints that clarify emerging directions. Background-only evidence includes broad surveys and non-peer-reviewed forecasting work that provide context but are not used as sole support for central claims.

This structure is used to keep novelty claims conservative and well-supported. A compact comparison map of key studies is provided in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:related-work-comparison}.

\subsection{Motorsport strategy simulation and planning}
Early motorsport strategy research established the value of fast simulation for decision support. \citet{heilmeier2018racesim} presented a lap-wise race simulation approach with tyre degradation, fuel effects, pit stops, and overtaking processes. A major strength of that work is pragmatic model design based on publicly accessible race data. A limitation is that the simulator primarily supports strategy evaluation rather than direct learning algorithm comparison across controlled seeds.

\citet{heilmeier2020virtual} extended strategy decision support through neural approximations and optimisation procedures. The study showed that data-driven surrogates can accelerate scenario exploration during race strategy planning. The work is strong on engineering relevance and practical deployment framing. It is less focused on reproducibility protocols for comparing learning agents under identical compute and stochastic settings.

\citet{piccinotti2021online} investigated online planning for F1 race strategy identification. This line of work demonstrates that planning methods can produce competitive decisions in dynamic race contexts. The limitation for this dissertation is methodological scope. Planning-focused studies typically do not provide DQN-family benchmarking with explicit seed variance reporting, which is central to the current aim.

Formula E strategy research adds another useful perspective. \citet{liu2020formulae} combined neural modelling with Monte Carlo tree search for energy strategy decisions. The work highlights how time-critical race strategy can be framed as multi-stage decision making with large state spaces. More recent multi-car Formula E work further supports the value of reinforcement learning in competitive settings \citep{liu2024formulae}. However, these studies usually target domain performance and decision quality in specific settings rather than a reproducibility-first benchmark protocol across multiple DQN variants.

Taken together, motorsport strategy papers show that simulation abstractions can produce meaningful tactical insight. They also show that tyre and energy dynamics matter for strategic realism. The remaining gap is an explicit evaluation framework where algorithm comparisons are controlled, statistically grounded, and interpretable for non-specialist readers.

\subsection{Reinforcement learning in competitive uncertain systems}
Reinforcement learning has long been framed as learning through interaction with uncertain environments \citep{ribeiro2002rlagents}. This framing is directly relevant to race strategy, where policies must handle uncertainty and opponent interaction rather than one fixed deterministic path.

\citet{groeneveld2023recommerce} provides a useful cross-domain example from recommerce markets. The paper shows that self-learning agents can adapt in competitive environments where uncertainty and strategic interaction both matter. The relevance is conceptual rather than domain-specific. It supports the case that benchmark design must expose methods to controlled uncertainty and competition to avoid overfitting to narrow deterministic conditions.

MARL literature gives a stronger theoretical basis for this framing. \citet{busoniu2008survey} identifies stochasticity, decentralisation, and interaction-driven non-stationarity as defining challenges in multi-agent reinforcement learning. \citet{hernandezleal2019survey} extends that critique into deep MARL and highlights why naive independent learners are difficult to evaluate fairly. \citet{foerster2017stabilising} shows that replay-based multi-agent learning becomes unstable when concurrent policy change is ignored, while \citet{foerster2018coma} demonstrates how CTDE can address multi-agent credit assignment. In value-based cooperative MARL, \citet{sunehag2018vdn} and \citet{rashid2018qmix} show how decentralised execution can be retained while training exploits centralised structure. Together, these studies justify the dissertation's decision to treat stochastic racing as a MARL problem first, and to position independent learners, CTDE, and value factorisation as a coherent progression rather than unrelated algorithm choices.

Recent motorsport-focused multi-agent work is also emerging. \citet{fieni2026learning} reports learning-based multi-agent race strategies in Formula 1 contexts and signals growing interest in this research direction. This is valuable as frontier context. It does not remove the need for transparent, reproducible benchmark design in smaller controlled simulators, especially for undergraduate project scope and timescale.

A non-peer-reviewed preprint on championship point forecasting illustrates wider machine learning interest in Formula 1 analytics \citep{bansal2025advanced}. The study offers descriptive context on data scale and forecasting ambition. It is not used as primary evidence for strategic control claims due to publication status and task mismatch.

\subsection{DQN-family foundations and method choice}
The DQN line is a suitable core for this dissertation because the simulator action space is tactical and discrete, and because value-based control provides a practical first implementation baseline for concurrent MARL learners. The original deep Q-network established that deep value learning can achieve strong control performance when combined with replay and target networks \citep{mnih2015human}. This provides the baseline reference for method comparison.

Double DQN addresses overestimation bias in Q-value targets, which can materially affect policy stability \citep{vanhasselt2016double}. Dueling network architectures separate state value from action advantage and can improve learning efficiency when many actions have similar effects \citep{wang2016dueling}. Prioritised replay improves sample efficiency by focusing learning on transitions with large temporal-difference error \citep{schaul2016prioritized}. Rainbow combines several DQN improvements into a single framework and demonstrates strong aggregate performance across benchmark tasks \citep{hessel2018rainbow}.

Action-space design literature further supports this methodological choice. \citet{zhu2021actionspaces} argues that algorithm suitability depends strongly on how action spaces are represented and constrained. Since this project uses a compact tactical decision space around overtaking opportunities, DQN-family methods form a coherent primary comparison set. Their role in this dissertation is therefore specific: they are the chosen value-based family used to instantiate the first MARL baseline and to support controlled comparison before stronger CTDE value-factorisation methods are introduced.

Policy-gradient and multi-agent actor-critic methods remained relevant context. PPO is widely used and robust in many settings, yet it was not the primary focus of this dissertation because the immediate goal was a controlled DQN-family benchmark \citep{schulman2017ppo}. MADDPG was treated as context for MARL training design rather than as the primary algorithmic comparison arm \citep{lowe2017maddpg}. This protected delivery quality while retaining technical ambition.

\subsection{Reproducible evaluation under uncertainty}
Methodological reliability is a central concern in modern reinforcement learning evaluation. \citet{henderson2018deep} showed that random seeds, implementation details, and reporting choices can materially change apparent conclusions. \citet{agarwal2021statistical} reinforced this point by showing how fragile rankings can be without statistically principled analysis.

These findings directly shape this dissertation design. Equal compute budgets and matched seeds are treated as mandatory controls rather than optional extras. Confidence intervals and across-seed variance are reported to avoid single-run claims. Behavioural diagnostics are included because outcome-only metrics can hide policy failure modes in competitive environments.

Work beyond reinforcement learning also supports robust scenario evaluation principles. \citet{sankary2018stochastic} studied stochastic scenario selection in evolutionary optimisation and demonstrated that uncertainty-aware evaluation procedures can improve robustness and reduce overfitting to narrow scenario sets. Although the optimisation paradigm differs, the methodological lesson transfers well to competitive race simulation.

\subsection{Synthesis of limitations and research gap}
The reviewed literature establishes three strong foundations. Motorsport studies provide practical simulation abstractions with strategic relevance. Reinforcement learning literature provides algorithmic mechanisms for learning under uncertainty. Reproducibility literature provides statistical discipline for trustworthy comparison.

A clear gap remains at their intersection. Few studies provide a reproducible MARL-oriented benchmark in a competitive motorsport-inspired simulator where stochastic factors are systematically varied, all-learning agents interact directly, and a transparent value-based comparator family is analysed alongside behavioural diagnostics.

This dissertation addressed that gap through three linked contributions. First, it framed race strategy explicitly as a stochastic MARL problem and analysed robustness under uncertainty. Second, it justified the DQN family as a principled value-based comparator set for this tactical multi-agent use-case, grounded in action-space fit, replay efficiency, and stabilisation mechanisms, while making the implementation path from independent learners to CTDE value factorisation explicit. Third, it enforced fairness-centred evaluation with matched budgets, fixed seeds, and interval-based reporting so conclusions remained statistically interpretable.

The chapter also resolved a common ambiguity in student projects that combine domain simulation with machine learning. It separated algorithm performance claims from simulator realism claims while keeping the investigation grounded in a realistic strategic interaction setting. RQ1 was addressed through robustness analysis under stochasticity. RQ2 was addressed through behavioural diagnostics linking interaction patterns to race outcomes. RQ3 was addressed through controlled, reproducible DQN-family comparisons inside the MARL framing.

The novelty claim is therefore methodological and evidential. The contribution is a reliable comparison framework and interpretable findings for an F1-inspired strategy setting, not a claim of solving multi-agent reinforcement learning for real Formula 1 operations.
