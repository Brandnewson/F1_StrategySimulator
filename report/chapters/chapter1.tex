\chapter{Introduction and Background Research}
\label{chapter1}

\section{Introduction}

In multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), agents that learn alongside each other do not just solve a task. They adapt to one another. Learning stability and emergent strategy both depend on how incentives are structured between agents, and that dependency is not well characterised. Much of MARL has historically focused on fully cooperative or fully competitive extremes, with many methods tailored to those cases. The partially-aligned or general-sum regime is an up-and-coming research area that remains less mature.

The question this project investigates is whether shared incentive between concurrently learning agents produces genuine cooperative advantage, or merely redistributes competitive outcomes. Studies in game theory make a prediction for one edge case. In a zero-sum interaction, an agent's gain is the other's loss by definition, so no reward formula can produce net joint improvement. That constraint lifts the moment a common adversary is introduced. Whether shared incentive actually exploits that opening, and how far it can be pushed before it destabilises learning rather than improving, has no conclusive answer in existing literature.

Formula 1 overtaking zone decisions are a well-suited environment to study this. Decisions are discrete and repeated, outcomes are probabilistic given the same zone and gap, and the opponent adapts across laps. The game structure is also adjustable by design. Two agents racing head-to-head form a zero-sum game. Introducing a fixed common adversary breaks that constraint and allows both agents to improve simultaneously. The same simulator, agents, and reward formula can be tested across both structures, isolating game structure as an independent variable. 

To answer this, the project builds a F1-inspired simulator and trains reinforcement learning (RL) agents across zero-sum and non-zero-sum game structures, with a reward-sharing coefficient varied systematically. To provide a scientific approach to the question, we draw from the fields of motorsport simulation, RL in competitive environments, and multi-agent incentive design.

\subsection{Motorsport simulation}

The earliest motorsport strategy research treats the simulator as infrastructure for strategy evaluation rather than algorithm comparison. A lap-wise simulation was built modelling tyre degradation, fuel effects, pit stops, and overtaking processes from publicly accessible race data \citep{heilmeier2018racesim}. The goal was to find a good strategy for a given race. That is a different problem from comparing how learning algorithms develop strategies under controlled conditions. The first requires environmental realism. The second requires experimental control. This work was extended with neural network surrogates showing that data-driven approximations can accelerate strategy exploration \citep{heilmeier2020virtual}, and online planning for Formula 1 confirmed that planning methods can produce competitive decisions in dynamic conditions \citep{piccinotti2021online}. These studies consistently evaluate decision quality against a fixed simulator rather than comparing agents that are learning simultaneously.

The Formula E literature narrows the gap. Neural networks combined with MCTS produced competitive single-car strategies \citep{liu2020formulae}, using fixed opponent models. A multi-car extension showed that RL works in competitive motorsport settings with more than one learning agent \citep{liu2024formulae}, but left open how incentive structure between agents shapes the strategies that emerge. Learning-based multi-agent race strategies in Formula 1 have also been reported \citep{fieni2026learning}.

Across this body of work, abstracted simulators produce meaningful tactical insight without full physical fidelity, and reinforcement learning is an established framework for racing strategy. No existing motorsport study has systematically varied the incentive structure between concurrent learning agents, or tested how reward alignment between agents changes the strategies that emerge.

\subsection{Reinforcement learning in competitive environments}

Reinforcement learning frames decision-making as an agent that observes a state, selects an action, and receives a reward. Across repeated episodes the agent adjusts its policy, the mapping from states to actions, to maximise long-term reward. Value-based methods estimate a Q-value for each action in each state, and select the action with the highest Q-value at each step \citep{mnih2015human}.

Applied to competitive settings, the framework has produced strong results over the past decade. AlphaGo and AlphaZero combined Monte Carlo tree search with deep networks trained through self-play, and reached superhuman performance in Go, chess, and shogi \citep{silver2017alphagozero, silver2018alphazero}. Libratus achieved approximate equilibrium in two-player no-limit poker through regret minimisation \citep{brown2018superhuman}. OpenAI Five trained policy gradients at scale to master Dota 2 \citep{berner2019dota2}, and AlphaStar used actor-critic with population-based training in StarCraft II \citep{vinyals2019alphastar}. Adversarial market simulations apply the same framework \citep{groeneveld2023recommerce}.

Three substrate families dominate modern RL. Value-based methods estimate Q-values and pick the highest-valued action, which suits small discrete action spaces. Policy-based methods learn a policy directly and scale well to continuous control, though they are less sample-efficient. Actor-critic combines both approaches and has become the default for continuous and large-scale domains. Discrete decisions with a modest compute budget suit value-based learning. Within that family, the Deep Q-Network (DQN) was the first to scale value-based learning to high-dimensional state spaces using deep neural networks \citep{mnih2015human}. Its variants form the substrate for this project, and the specific one used is justified in Section~\ref{sec:dqn-foundations}.


The central challenge in competitive RL is that a policy learned against a fixed opponent degrades when that opponent adapts. Self-play addresses this by training an agent against increasingly skilled versions of itself. It introduces its own failure modes, including cyclic policy dynamics and exploitability by novel opponents. Self-play is a single-agent method adapted to adversarial settings. This project trains two agents concurrently, which is the setting of multi-agent reinforcement learning.

\subsection{Multi-agent incentive design}

In multi-agent settings, outcomes depend on joint actions rather than individual ones, and agents observe only local state \citep{busoniu2008survey}. Because all agents update simultaneously, the environment any one agent experiences shifts as the others' policies change. This effect is known as 'non-stationarity', and it is the main technical challenge in multi-agent learning \citep{hernandezleal2019survey}.

Game structure determines what improvement is even possible. In a zero-sum game one agent's gain is the other's loss. In a non-zero-sum game both agents can improve simultaneously by outperforming a common adversary. General-sum games, where interests are partially compatible and partially conflicting, sit between these two poles \citep{hu2003nash, lowe2017maddpg}. Mixed-motive settings remain less developed than the pure cooperative and zero-sum cases in benchmarking and realistic domains \citep{du2023cooperation}, and in motorsport this space is only recently emerging \citep{fieni2026learning}.

Independent learners are the natural first baseline. Each agent maintains its own replay buffer, which fills with transitions from the opponent's past policy \citep{foerster2017stabilising}. When that policy changes, stored experience misdirects updates and learning can become unstable \citep{matignon2012independent}. Independent learners can outperform centralised agents at low agent counts, but performance degrades as count grows \citep{tan1993multiagent}. Characterising the specific collapse mode is a prerequisite for the incentive structure analysis, since different alpha values could produce different collapse rates rather than different strategies.

Non-stationarity intensifies with agent count as more concurrently updating policies shift the target distribution, and credit assignment gets harder as more agents contribute to each joint outcome \citep{agogino2004unifying, zhao2025maca}. How shared-incentive schemes interact with agent count remains less well characterised.

Credit assignment is a central unsolved problem in team reward settings \citep{panait2005cooperative, ningxie2024marl}. It is the problem of distributing a shared team reward among agents in proportion to their individual contribution. Two broad families of solution exist at the level of the reward signal. The first mixes individual and team rewards to balance credit assignment against cooperative alignment \citep{wang2022irat}, which is the mechanism the alpha coefficient implements. The second subtracts a counterfactual baseline from each agent's reward, isolating that agent's individual contribution to the team outcome. Difference rewards are the canonical form of this approach \citep{wolpert2001optimal, devlin2014potential}. Exact computation requires evaluating the team outcome under every agent's hypothetical absence, which is impractical at scale, so mean-field approximations that substitute the observed team mean are commonly used \citep{yang2018meanfield}. The observed team mean is not the counterfactual outcome under agent $i$'s hypothetical absence, and approximations that conflate the two can lose the alignment property that difference rewards rely on \citep{devlin2014potential}.

A separate line of work addresses credit assignment at the level of training architecture rather than the reward signal. Centralised training with decentralised execution shares global information during training while keeping execution local, which enables joint-value decomposition in QMIX \citep{rashid2018qmix} and counterfactual policy gradients in COMA \citep{foerster2018coma}.

These responses map onto two distinct failure modes. Training-order collapse arises when cooperative reward is introduced before individual policies have stabilised, overwriting competent baselines with passivity \citep{matignon2012independent}. Credit-assignment failure arises when overlapping individual contributions prevent team reward from producing useful per-agent gradients \citep{panait2005cooperative, rashid2018qmix}. Distinguishing the two in practice requires a diagnostic that varies incentive alignment across training rather than holding it fixed.

\section{The niche: social strategy and incentive structure}
\label{sec:social-dilemmas}

\subsection{Social dilemmas and the emergence of cooperation}

A social dilemma is a situation where individually rational choices produce a collectively worse outcome than cooperation would \citep{leibo2017multiagent}. In sequential social dilemmas, independent learners frequently converge to defection even when cooperation would produce higher collective reward.

In this simulator, the dilemma appears when two agents both target the easiest overtaking zone simultaneously. Each reduces the other's opportunity, a racing instance of the tragedy of the commons, where shared resources are depleted by individually rational use \citep{nowak2006five}. Giving agents partial credit for a teammate's reward substantially increases cooperative behaviour \citep{hughes2018inequity}. When competing aggressively at the teammate's zone becomes costly to the self, agents develop complementary territory and reduce direct competition.
Direct reciprocity, where agents sustain cooperation by consistently responding to each other's behaviour across repeated interactions \citep{nowak2006five}, is the most applicable mechanism here. Agents that repeatedly face the same opponent can sustain cooperation through best-response dynamics, and partial incentive alignment makes this structurally attractive even when reciprocity signals are noisy.

\subsection{The reward-sharing coefficient as a research variable}

Alpha is the primary experimental variable. It controls how much each agent weights a teammate's outcome alongside its own. At alpha zero, agents are purely competitive. At alpha one, agents are fully cooperative. At intermediate values, individual incentive is moderated by partial concern for the teammate's result.

Changing alpha is predicted to change the qualitative character of the strategies that emerge \citep{hughes2018inequity,nowak2006five}. It should affect how agents partition territory across zones and whether their risk profiles diverge, with those patterns becoming more or less stable as the environment grows noisier.

\section{DQN substrate as the measurement apparatus}
\label{sec:dqn-foundations}

The decision space in this simulator is discrete. At each zone, an agent holds or attempts an overtake at one of three risk levels. Q-value methods maintain separate estimates for each action, which fits this structure \citep{zhu2021actionspaces, mnih2015human}.
Off-policy replay, where an agent learns from stored past transitions rather than only from the most recent episode, is attractive here because it allows repeated learning from rare but informative overtaking events \citep{mnih2015human, schaul2016prioritized}. The comparison set therefore consists of vanilla DQN, Double DQN, Dueling DQN, and a Rainbow-style variant combining these with prioritised experience replay \citep{hessel2018rainbow}. Prioritised replay weights training toward transitions with the highest temporal-difference error rather than sampling uniformly from the buffer \citep{schaul2016prioritized}, which matters when informative overtaking events are rare in the replay. In the multi-agent setting, however, replay must be interpreted carefully. Independent learners induce non-stationarity in the data distribution, so transitions stored against an earlier opponent policy may misdirect updates once that policy shifts \citep{foerster2017stabilising}.

Vanilla Q-learning inflates action values in stochastic environments because the Bellman max operator is biased upward under variance. Noisy overtake outcomes may inflate Q-values for aggressive actions, biasing agents toward overconfident risk-taking. Double DQN corrects this by separating action selection from value evaluation in the target \citep{vanhasselt2016double}. The dueling architecture separates state value from action advantage \citep{wang2016dueling}. This prevents the advantage head from noisily differentiating actions with near-identical expected outcomes, which is common at zones where actions are often near-indistinguishable in value.

Comparing variants within the same family under identical infrastructure better isolates the effect of each mechanism. Differences in emergent strategy across alpha values are more attributable to incentive structure when algorithm-level differences are controlled \citep{henderson2018deep, agarwal2021statistical}. Independent Q-learning remains a strong and commonly applied baseline in MARL for value-based settings \citep{rashid2018qmix}. Identifying which variant is resistant to collapse in the competitive baseline is a prerequisite for the incentive-regime comparison.

\section{The research gap and this project's contribution}

No existing motorsport study has varied incentive structure between concurrently learning agents in a stochastic simulator. No existing MARL study has measured whether such variation produces genuine cooperative advantage or merely redistributes competitive outcomes.

This project fills that gap by characterising where and why cooperative incentive helps, harms, and breaks learning. A reward-sharing coefficient is varied across zero-sum and non-zero-sum game structures to establish what game structure alone permits. Given both non-stationarity and credit assignment worsen with agent count, a count sweep identifies a sharp boundary at which cooperative incentive transitions from beneficial to harmful. Curriculum scheduling over alpha then separates two distinct failure modes within that breakdown.


\section{Aim, objectives, and research questions}

\subsection{Aim}

Multi-agent learning has been studied extensively in fully cooperative and fully competitive settings. The space between, where self-interested agents are only partially aligned, is recognised in the literature but remains less mature in its benchmarks, evaluation metrics, and realistic testbeds. The aim of this project is to study how cooperation between self-interested learning agents changes as incentive alignment is varied from pure conflict toward partial alignment, and to test whether the different ways cooperation breaks down reflect a shared underlying mechanism or distinct causes.

\subsection{Objectives}

The project has four objectives.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Design and internally validate a stochastic racing simulator with reproducible seeding, configuration-controlled complexity profiles spanning game structure, agent count, and stochasticity, and diagnostic instrumentation sufficient to distinguish cooperative advantage from redistributed competitive outcomes.
    \item Identify a DQN-family variant that provides sufficiently stable single-agent learning to serve as a controlled substrate for the incentive regime comparison.
    \item Characterise how varying the reward-sharing coefficient affects emergent strategy, learning stability, and cooperative performance across game structures and agent counts.
    \item Evaluate difference rewards as an alternative cooperative formulation and test whether a mean-field approximation preserves incentive alignment with the team objective \citep{wolpert2001optimal}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Research questions and hypotheses}

\textbf{RQ1.} What patterns of strategic behaviour emerge when independent reinforcement learning agents train concurrently under pure competition?

\textbf{H1.} Concurrent training should produce asymmetric risk preferences and differentiated zone usage through iterated best-response dynamics. Neither pattern should require explicit coordination.

\textbf{RQ2.} How does varying the reward-sharing coefficient alter the emergent strategy profile, and does a threshold exist above which shared incentive starts destabilising learning?

\textbf{H2.} Beyond a critical threshold the cooperative term should dominate individual gradients, inverting the incentive to improve own position and driving agents toward passive equilibria.

\textbf{RQ3.} Does shared incentive produce genuine cooperative advantage through improved joint performance against a common adversary, or does it just redistribute outcomes within a zero-sum interaction?

\textbf{H3.} In a zero-sum interaction shared incentive cannot produce genuine cooperative advantage. Introducing a common adversary breaks this constraint. Under non-zero-sum conditions, partial incentive alignment should increase joint performance by encouraging zone differentiation against the adversary.

\textbf{RQ4.} Do alternative credit assignment formulations preserve the cooperative advantage observed under alpha-based reward mixing?

\textbf{H4.} Credit assignment formulations that rely on approximating a counterfactual baseline are predicted to break cooperative alignment, and the advantage observed under alpha-based mixing should not reproduce under such approximations.
