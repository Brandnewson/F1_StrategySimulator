\chapter{Introduction and Background Research}
\label{chapter1}

\section{Introduction}

Formula 1 race positions change at overtaking zones. At each zone, a driver decides whether to attack and at what risk level. The decision depends on the gap to the car ahead, available tyre life, and how the opponent has behaved on earlier laps. No fixed policy covers all cases, because what succeeds against one opponent often fails against another.

This project focuses on micro-decisions at individual braking zones. Macro-level concerns such as pit stop timing and tyre selection are held outside scope to keep the dynamics under study interpretable.

A preliminary investigation confirmed that RL agents can learn overtaking decisions and that different algorithmic variants show meaningfully different reliability under stochastic outcomes. Both findings assume a fixed opponent. Once the opponent is also learning, each agent's effective environment shifts as the other's policy evolves, and non-stationarity becomes the central challenge rather than any individual algorithmic limitation.

A further question concerns whether the reward contract itself shapes the strategies that emerge. An agent rewarded purely for its own finishing position has different incentives from one that also accounts for a teammate's outcome. Varying the degree of this alignment changes the qualitative character of the strategies that develop. The relationship between incentive structure and emergent strategy is the central research question.

\section{Reinforcement learning in complex environments}

The form of RL used here is value-based learning, where the agent maintains Q-value estimates for every action in every state. An agent with accurate Q-values selects actions by looking ahead rather than maximising immediate return \citep{mnih2015human}, which matters because an aggressive overtake attempt may succeed in the moment but cost more than it recovers in position over the remaining race distance.

The Deep Q-Network (DQN) family approximates Q-values using deep neural networks, with the original DQN showing that this approach scales to a range of complex environments \citep{mnih2015human}. Double DQN corrects systematic Q-value overestimation under noisy rewards by separating action selection from value evaluation in the Bellman target \citep{vanhasselt2016double}. Dueling DQN decomposes the estimate into a state-value and an action-advantage head, improving learning in states where the choice of action has little effect on the expected outcome \citep{wang2016dueling}. Prioritised experience replay focuses training on the highest-TD-error transitions rather than drawing uniformly from the buffer \citep{schaul2016prioritized}. Rainbow combines these mechanisms into a single algorithm \citep{hessel2018rainbow}. All four variants form the algorithmic comparison set for this project, with the full justification for this selection in Section~\ref{sec:dqn-foundations}.

Single-agent reinforcement learning assumes a stationary environment where only the agent's own policy changes between episodes. Multi-agent settings break this assumption: when several agents learn simultaneously, each agent's effective environment is shaped by the others' evolving policies, so an action that produced a high reward may produce a different result once the opponent adapts. This non-stationarity is the core technical challenge of MARL \citep{busoniu2008survey,hernandezleal2019survey}.

Q-values trained against one opponent policy become inaccurate once that policy shifts. Since all agents update simultaneously the target each agent learns against never stabilises, and learning can collapse rather than converge \citep{foerster2017stabilising,matignon2012independent}.

\subsection{Motorsport simulation and planning}

The earliest motorsport strategy research treats the simulator as infrastructure for strategy evaluation rather than algorithm comparison. A lap-wise simulation was built modelling tyre degradation, fuel effects, pit stops, and overtaking processes from publicly accessible race data \citep{heilmeier2018racesim}. The goal was to find a good strategy for a given race. That is a different problem from comparing how learning algorithms develop strategies under controlled conditions. The first requires environmental realism. The second requires experimental control. This work was extended with neural network surrogates showing that data-driven approximations can accelerate strategy exploration \citep{heilmeier2020virtual}, and online planning for Formula 1 confirmed that planning methods can produce competitive decisions in dynamic conditions \citep{piccinotti2021online}. These studies consistently evaluate decision quality against a fixed simulator rather than comparing agents that are learning simultaneously.

The Formula E literature narrows the gap. Neural networks combined with MCTS produced competitive single-car strategies \citep{liu2020formulae}, using fixed opponent models. A multi-car extension showed that RL works in competitive motorsport settings with more than one learning agent \citep{liu2024formulae}, but left open how incentive structure between agents shapes the strategies that emerge. Learning-based multi-agent race strategies in Formula 1 have also been reported \citep{fieni2026learning}.

Across this body of work, abstracted simulators produce meaningful tactical insight without full physical fidelity, and reinforcement learning is an established framework for racing strategy. No existing motorsport study has systematically varied the incentive structure between concurrent learning agents, or tested how reward alignment between agents changes the strategies that emerge.

\subsection{Reinforcement learning in competitive environments}

Environmental uncertainty comes from stochastic overtake outcomes and strategic uncertainty from the opponent. Keeping these two sources separable is a design requirement: the stochasticity levels control environmental uncertainty directly, and the alpha sweep controls the incentive regime. Testing under a single fixed condition overstates policy quality and hides failure modes that only appear when conditions shift \citep{groeneveld2023recommerce}.

Seeds, budgets, and reporting conventions can reverse algorithm rankings \citep{henderson2018deep}. Confidence intervals across seeds are necessary \citep{agarwal2021statistical}, and varied stochastic evaluation distinguishes generalisable findings from narrow artefacts \citep{sankary2018stochastic}. Three stochasticity levels are therefore tested throughout rather than one.

\subsection{Multi-agent reinforcement learning}

In multi-agent settings, outcomes depend on joint actions rather than individual ones, and agents observe only local state \citep{busoniu2008survey}. Because all agents update simultaneously, the environment any one agent experiences is non-stationary throughout training. The more open question is how these properties interact with incentive structure. That interaction is what this project investigates.

The literature largely treats the competitive-cooperative distinction as binary \citep{hernandezleal2019survey}. Purely competitive agents operate in zero-sum games where equilibria are defined by game theory. Purely cooperative agents share a joint reward and face a coordination problem. The mixed setting, where agents are partially aligned, is acknowledged but systematically underexplored. The question of what happens as incentive alignment is tuned between these two poles has not been studied in a motorsport context. It is only beginning to be addressed in MARL more broadly.

Independent learners can outperform centralised agents at moderate agent counts, but performance degrades as agent count grows \citep{tan1993multiagent}. Credit assignment is the central unsolved problem in team reward settings \citep{panait2005cooperative}. Individual and team rewards can be mixed to balance credit assignment against cooperative alignment \citep{wang2022irat}, which is the mechanism the alpha coefficient implements in this project.

Using independent Q-learners in a multi-agent setting is a deliberate baseline. Each agent's replay buffer fills with transitions from the opponent's past policy \citep{foerster2017stabilising}. When that policy changes, the stored experience misdirects updates and learning can become unstable in several distinct ways \citep{matignon2012independent}. Characterising the specific collapse mode in this setting is a prerequisite for the incentive structure analysis, since different alpha values could produce different collapse rates rather than different strategies.

Centralised training with decentralised execution (CTDE) is the standard architectural response to non-stationarity, with QMIX \citep{rashid2018qmix} and COMA \citep{foerster2018coma} using global information during training while preserving local execution. This project characterises the independent learner failure modes first, establishing where and why CTDE methods become necessary.

\section{The niche: social strategy and incentive structure}
\label{sec:social-dilemmas}

\subsection{Social dilemmas and the emergence of cooperation}

A social dilemma is a situation where individually rational choices produce a collectively worse outcome than cooperation would \citep{leibo2017multiagent}. In sequential social dilemmas, independent learners frequently converge to defection even when cooperation would produce higher collective reward.

In this simulator, the dilemma appears when two agents both target the easiest overtaking zone simultaneously, reducing each other's opportunity in a racing version of the tragedy of the commons \citep{nowak2006five}. Giving agents partial credit for a teammate's reward substantially increases cooperative behaviour \citep{hughes2018inequity}: when competing aggressively at the teammate's zone becomes costly to the self, agents develop complementary territory and reduce direct competition for both.

Direct reciprocity is the most relevant cooperation mechanism here: agents that repeatedly face the same opponent can sustain cooperation through best-response dynamics, and partial incentive alignment makes this structurally attractive even when reciprocity signals are noisy \citep{nowak2006five}.

\subsection{The reward-sharing coefficient as a research variable}

Alpha is the primary experimental variable. It controls how much each agent weights a teammate's outcome alongside its own. At alpha zero, agents are purely competitive. At alpha one, agents are fully cooperative. At intermediate values, individual incentive is moderated by partial concern for the teammate's result.

Changing alpha is predicted to change the qualitative character of the strategies that emerge \citep{hughes2018inequity,nowak2006five}. It should affect how agents partition territory across zones and whether their risk profiles diverge, with those patterns becoming more or less stable as the environment grows noisier.

\subsection{Why the DQN family is the right substrate}
\label{sec:dqn-foundations}

The DQN family suits this comparison for several connected reasons. The decision space in this simulator is discrete. At each zone, an agent holds or attempts an overtake at one of three risk levels. Q-value methods maintain separate estimates for each action, which fits this structure \citep{zhu2021actionspaces,mnih2015human}.

Experience replay also matters in a five-lap race with sparse decisions. Value-based methods can reuse past transitions, learning from rare but informative events more efficiently than on-policy methods that discard experience after a single update \citep{mnih2015human,schaul2016prioritized}.

The Q-value overestimation problem is directly relevant. Vanilla Q-learning inflates action values in stochastic environments because the Bellman max operator is biased upward under variance. Double DQN corrects this by separating action selection from value evaluation in the target \citep{vanhasselt2016double}. Noisy overtake outcomes inflate Q-values for aggressive actions, biasing agents toward overconfident risk-taking in stochastic conditions.

The dueling architecture reduces noise in states where holding is almost always correct. Separating state value from action advantage \citep{wang2016dueling} prevents the advantage head from noisily differentiating actions that have near-identical expected outcomes.

Comparing variants within the same family under identical infrastructure isolates the effect of each mechanism. Differences in emergent strategy across alpha values must be attributable to incentive structure, not to algorithm-level differences \citep{henderson2018deep,agarwal2021statistical}. DQN-family independent learners are the standard first baseline in the MARL literature for value-based settings \citep{hernandezleal2019survey}. Identifying which variant is resistant to collapse in the competitive baseline is therefore a prerequisite for the incentive-regime comparison.

\section{The research gap and this project's contribution}

No existing motorsport study has varied the incentive structure between concurrently learning agents in a stochastic simulator, and no existing MARL study has measured whether such variation produces genuine cooperative advantage or merely redistributes competitive outcomes. Neither question has been combined with a matched-budget evaluation protocol reporting confidence intervals and behavioural diagnostics alongside raw win rate.

This project addresses that gap. Race strategy is framed as a social dilemma where incentive structure determines emergent behaviour. A reward-sharing coefficient spanning purely competitive to fully cooperative incentives is tested across zero-sum and non-zero-sum game structures. An agent-count ablation identifies a sharp scaling boundary. Curriculum scheduling separates two distinct failure modes within that breakdown. A mathematical derivation shows that the mean-field approximation of difference rewards \citep{wolpert2002optimal} inverts the cooperative gradient. A zero-shot LLM provides a non-learning comparator.


\section{Aim, objectives, and research questions}

\subsection{Aim}

This project uses a stochastic F1-inspired racing simulator to study how varying a reward-sharing coefficient between competing DQN-family agents changes the strategies they develop, whether partial alignment produces genuine cooperative advantage or learning collapse, and how these effects change as agent count increases.

\subsection{Objectives}

The project has four objectives. The first is to design and validate a stochastic racing simulator with configurable complexity, reproducible seeding, and structured behavioural telemetry. The second is to identify which DQN-family variant provides a stable learning substrate for the incentive regime comparison. The third is to characterise how varying the reward sharing coefficient affects emergent strategy, learning stability, and cooperative performance across multiple stochasticity levels and agent counts. The fourth is to evaluate difference rewards as an alternative cooperative formulation and establish whether a mean-field approximation preserves the cooperative gradient property \citep{wolpert2002optimal}.

\subsection{Research questions and hypotheses}

\textbf{RQ1.} What patterns of strategic behaviour emerge when independent reinforcement learning agents train concurrently in a shared stochastic environment, and how sensitive are those patterns to environmental noise?

\textbf{H1.} Concurrent training is predicted to produce asymmetric risk preferences and differentiated zone usage through iterated best-response dynamics. Higher stochasticity is expected to weaken the Q-value signal for secondary zones and reduce the reliability of stable specialisation.

\textbf{RQ2.} How does systematically varying the reward sharing coefficient between agents alter the emergent strategy profile, and is there a threshold beyond which shared incentive destabilises rather than improves learning?

\textbf{H2.} Beyond a critical threshold the cooperative term is predicted to dominate individual gradients, inverting the incentive to improve own position and producing convergence to passive equilibria. This transition is predicted to be sharp rather than gradual. Whether the threshold shifts with agent count, and whether curriculum scheduling can recover lost performance, are sub-questions that follow from establishing the threshold's existence.

\textbf{RQ3.} Does shared incentive produce genuine cooperative advantage, measured as improved joint performance against a common adversary, or does it merely redistribute outcomes within a fixed interaction?

\textbf{H3.} In a zero-sum interaction shared incentive cannot produce genuine cooperative advantage. Introducing a common adversary breaks this constraint. Under non-zero-sum conditions, partial incentive alignment is predicted to increase joint performance by encouraging zone differentiation, though fully cooperative incentive may restore pathological convergence if the individual gradient becomes too weak.
