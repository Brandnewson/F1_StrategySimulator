\chapter{Introduction and Background Research}
\label{chapter1}

\section{Introduction}

Formula 1 race positions change at overtaking zones. At each zone, a driver decides whether to attack and at what risk level. The decision depends on the gap to the car ahead, available tyre life, and how the opponent has behaved on earlier laps. No fixed policy covers all cases, because what succeeds against one opponent often fails against another.

Macro decisions (pit stop timing, tyre selection) play out over the full race duration. Micro decisions happen at individual braking zones each lap. This project focuses on the micro scale. Macro-level concerns are held outside scope deliberately. A full-fidelity simulation would introduce too many interacting variables for the behavioural effects of interest to be isolated.

A preliminary investigation confirmed that reinforcement learning agents can learn overtaking decisions, and that different algorithmic variants show meaningfully different reliability when race outcomes are stochastic. Both findings, however, assume a fixed opponent. Once the opponent is also learning, each agent's effective environment shifts as the other's policy evolves, and what succeeded in one episode may fail in the next simply because the opponent has since adapted. It is this non-stationarity, rather than any individual algorithmic limitation, that is the central focus of the project.

A further question concerns whether the reward contract itself shapes the strategies that emerge. An agent rewarded purely for its own finishing position has different incentives from one that also accounts for a teammate's outcome, and varying the degree of this alignment changes the qualitative character of the strategies that develop, not merely how quickly agents converge. The relationship between incentive structure and emergent strategy is the central research question.

This chapter introduces the algorithmic family used, surveys relevant prior work, identifies the research gap, and states the research questions.

\section{Reinforcement learning in complex environments}

Reinforcement learning is a framework in which an agent learns a policy by taking actions and receiving reward signals. Over many interactions, the agent adjusts its behaviour to maximise cumulative reward \citep{ribeiro2002rlagents}.

There is no fixed correct overtaking policy that can be specified in advance. The right choice depends on the gap to the car ahead, relative speeds, and how conditions are likely to change over the remaining laps. These factors combine in too many ways for a hand-coded rule set to cover. Reinforcement learning allows an agent to build a policy from experience across many races.

The form used here is value-based learning, where the agent maintains an estimate of the expected long-run reward for every action available in every state. An agent with accurate estimates, called Q-values, selects actions by looking ahead rather than maximising immediate return \citep{mnih2015human}, which matters because an aggressive overtake attempt may succeed in the moment but cost more tyre life than it recovers in position. A deeper Q-value function accounts for these downstream consequences across the remaining race distance.

The Deep Q-Network (DQN) family approximates Q-values using deep neural networks, with the original DQN showing that this approach scales to a range of complex environments \citep{mnih2015human}. Double DQN corrects systematic Q-value overestimation under noisy rewards by separating action selection from value evaluation in the Bellman target \citep{vanhasselt2016double}. Dueling DQN decomposes the estimate into a state-value and an action-advantage head, improving learning in states where the choice of action has little effect on the expected outcome \citep{wang2016dueling}. Prioritised experience replay focuses training on the highest-TD-error transitions rather than drawing uniformly from the buffer \citep{schaul2016prioritized}. Rainbow combines these mechanisms into a single algorithm \citep{hessel2018rainbow}. All four variants form the algorithmic comparison set for this project, with the full justification for this selection in Section~\ref{sec:dqn-foundations}.

Single-agent reinforcement learning assumes a stationary environment, where the only thing changing between episodes is the agent's own policy, so a strategy that works well once can be expected to generalise to the same conditions.

Multi-agent reinforcement learning breaks this assumption. When several agents learn simultaneously, each agent's effective environment is shaped by the others' evolving policies, so an action that produced a high reward previously may produce a different result now simply because the opponent has adapted. This non-stationarity is the core technical challenge of multi-agent reinforcement learning \citep{busoniu2008survey,hernandezleal2019survey}.

Non-stationarity creates a specific problem for Q-value estimation, because Q-values trained against one opponent policy become inaccurate once that policy shifts, and since all agents update simultaneously the target each agent is learning against never stabilises. The result is that learning can destabilise, with policies cycling or collapsing rather than converging \citep{foerster2017stabilising,matignon2012independent}.

In a racing context, a driver who always attacks the same corner becomes predictable to an opponent that is learning, whilst one who varies their approach preserves strategic ambiguity. When both drivers are adapting simultaneously, the question is whether their policies settle into stable complementary strategies or whether mutual adaptation produces collapse instead.

\subsection{Motorsport simulation and planning}

Any motorsport simulator must decide at what resolution to model the race. The lap-by-lap approach represents each car by a base lap time and computes gaps and positions at the end of each lap. This is computationally cheap and captures pit stop and tyre strategy cleanly. The quasi-static approach uses a coordinate-space vehicle model to predict lap time from car setup and track geometry. This gives finer physical realism but requires many parameters calibrated from telemetry. Neither approach models the stochastic, contact-driven nature of overtaking directly. The approach taken here uses the lap-by-lap structure and adds an overtaking zone layer where success is a stochastic function of zone difficulty and risk level. This keeps the simulation lean enough for controlled agent comparisons while preserving the competitive interaction under study.

The earliest motorsport strategy research treats the simulator as infrastructure for strategy evaluation rather than algorithm comparison. A lap-wise simulation was built modelling tyre degradation, fuel effects, pit stops, and overtaking processes from publicly accessible race data \citep{heilmeier2018racesim}. The goal was to find a good strategy for a given race. That is a different problem from comparing how learning algorithms develop strategies under controlled conditions. The first requires environmental realism. The second requires experimental control. This work was extended with neural network surrogates showing that data-driven approximations can accelerate strategy exploration \citep{heilmeier2020virtual}, and online planning for Formula 1 confirmed that planning methods can produce competitive decisions in dynamic conditions \citep{piccinotti2021online}. These studies consistently evaluate decision quality against a fixed simulator rather than comparing agents that are learning simultaneously.

The Formula E literature narrows the gap. Neural networks were combined with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to develop race strategy for a single car \citep{liu2020formulae}. The problem is framed as a sequential decision problem under energy resource constraints. A car must decide when to deploy the Attack Mode power boost and how aggressively to manage energy across each stage. The neural network predicts opponent lap times, and MCTS searches over strategy sequences to maximise finishing position. The work shows that lap-by-lap decision-making under resource constraints can be formalised with learned components. The opponent models are fixed, however, so the strategic interaction reduces to prediction rather than genuine competition between learning agents. This gap was addressed by extending the approach to a multi-car setting with multiple cars competing simultaneously \citep{liu2024formulae}. Multiple cars now interact through shared track resources, and one car's strategy directly affects another's feasible options. The study shows that reinforcement learning works in competitive motorsport settings with more than one learning agent. What it leaves open is how the incentive structure between agents shapes the strategies that emerge. Learning-based multi-agent race strategies in Formula 1 have also been reported \citep{fieni2026learning}.

Across this body of work, abstracted simulators produce meaningful tactical insight without full physical fidelity, and reinforcement learning is an established framework for racing strategy. No existing motorsport study has systematically varied the incentive structure between concurrent learning agents, or tested how reward alignment between agents changes the strategies that emerge.

\subsection{Reinforcement learning in competitive environments}

Environmental uncertainty comes from stochastic overtake outcomes. The same action at the same zone does not always succeed. Strategic uncertainty comes from the opponent. A policy tuned to one opponent behaviour may fail against another. These two sources interact, and keeping them separable is a design requirement for this project. The stochasticity levels in the simulator control environmental uncertainty directly. The alpha sweep controls the incentive regime. Neither can be interpreted cleanly if they are confounded.

This is illustrated in competitive market settings, where agents trained against fixed opponents fail when opponents adapt \citep{groeneveld2023recommerce}. Testing under a single fixed condition overstates policy quality and hides failure modes that only appear when conditions shift. An incentive structure finding that holds only under deterministic overtake outcomes applies only to that noise environment.

Seeds, budgets, and reporting conventions can reverse algorithm rankings \citep{henderson2018deep}. Mean-only reporting is unreliable and confidence intervals across seeds are necessary \citep{agarwal2021statistical}. Matched compute budgets and fixed seeds are therefore mandatory controls. Interval-based reporting replaces mean-only summaries. Varied stochastic evaluation distinguishes generalisable findings from narrow artefacts \citep{sankary2018stochastic}. Three stochasticity levels are therefore tested throughout rather than one.

\subsection{Multi-agent reinforcement learning}

In multi-agent settings, outcomes depend on joint actions rather than individual ones, and agents observe only local state \citep{busoniu2008survey}. Because all agents update simultaneously, the environment any one agent experiences is non-stationary throughout training. The more open question is how these properties interact with incentive structure. That interaction is what this project investigates.

The literature largely treats the competitive-cooperative distinction as binary \citep{hernandezleal2019survey}. Purely competitive agents operate in zero-sum games where equilibria are defined by game theory. Purely cooperative agents share a joint reward and face a coordination problem. The mixed setting, where agents are partially aligned, is acknowledged but systematically underexplored. The question of what happens as incentive alignment is tuned between these two poles has not been studied in a motorsport context. It is only beginning to be addressed in MARL more broadly.

Independent learners can outperform centralised agents at moderate agent counts, but performance degrades as agent count grows \citep{tan1993multiagent}. Credit assignment is the central unsolved problem in team reward settings \citep{panait2005cooperative}. Individual and team rewards can be mixed to balance credit assignment against cooperative alignment \citep{wang2022irat}, which is the mechanism the alpha coefficient implements in this project.

Using independent Q-learners in a multi-agent setting is a deliberate baseline. Each agent's replay buffer fills with transitions from the opponent's past policy \citep{foerster2017stabilising}. When that policy changes, the stored experience misdirects updates. Several convergence pathologies follow from this \citep{matignon2012independent}. In this project, the failure takes a specific form. The trailing agent's buffer saturates with failed attempts, Q(HOLD) rises above Q(ATTEMPT) across all zones, and the agent converges to permanent passivity. Understanding this collapse is necessary before any incentive structure finding can be trusted. If different alpha values produce different collapse rates rather than different strategies, the comparison is confounded.

Centralised training with decentralised execution is the standard architectural response to non-stationarity. Counterfactual Multi-Agent Policy Gradients (COMA) \citep{foerster2018coma} and Q-value Mixing (QMIX) \citep{rashid2018qmix} use global information during training while keeping local execution. This has been extended to mixed cooperative-competitive settings, showing that centralised critics are necessary even when incentives are only partially shared \citep{lowe2017maddpg}. These methods point to where the architecture needs to go once the independent learner failure modes are characterised. This project characterises those failure modes first.

\section{The niche: social strategy and incentive structure}
\label{sec:social-dilemmas}

The prior work has not examined how incentive structure between concurrent learners shapes the strategies that emerge. That is the specific gap this project addresses.

\subsection{Social dilemmas and the emergence of cooperation}

A social dilemma is a situation where the individually rational choice produces a collectively worse outcome than cooperation would \citep{leibo2017multiagent}. The prisoner's dilemma illustrates this. Two suspects are questioned separately. If both stay silent, both receive a light sentence. If one confesses and the other stays silent, the confessor goes free. If both confess, both receive a moderate sentence. Confessing is the individually rational choice regardless of what the other does. When both follow this logic, both end up worse off than if they had stayed silent.

Sequential social dilemmas arise naturally when agents interact repeatedly in shared environments with overlapping resource interests \citep{leibo2017multiagent}. Independent learners frequently converge to defection even when cooperation would produce higher collective reward.

Two cars from the same team face a direct version of this. Both drivers want to finish as high as possible. The team benefits from both finishing well. If one car is faster but sits behind the other, the team may prefer a position swap. The leading driver's individual incentive is to hold. Formula 1 teams manage this through team orders and driver contracts.

In simulation, the same dilemma appears when two agents both target the easiest overtaking zone every lap. They reduce each other's opportunity by competing for the same resource. Both could do better if each committed to a different zone. This is the tragedy of the commons in a racing context \citep{nowak2006five}.

Giving agents partial credit for a teammate's reward substantially increases cooperative behaviour \citep{hughes2018inequity}. When competing aggressively at the teammate's zone becomes costly to the self, agents develop complementary territory. One takes the primary zone and the other develops a secondary, reducing direct competition for both.

Cooperation in interacting populations is sustained through kin selection, direct reciprocity, indirect reciprocity, network reciprocity, and group selection \citep{nowak2006five}. The most relevant here is direct reciprocity. Agents that repeatedly face the same opponent can sustain cooperation through best-response dynamics. Partial incentive alignment makes cooperation structurally attractive even when reciprocity signals are noisy.

\subsection{The reward-sharing coefficient as a research variable}

Alpha is the primary experimental variable. It controls how much each agent weights a teammate's outcome alongside its own. At alpha zero, agents are purely competitive. At alpha one, agents are fully cooperative. At intermediate values, individual incentive is moderated by partial concern for the teammate's result.

Changing alpha is predicted to change the qualitative character of the strategies that emerge \citep{hughes2018inequity,nowak2006five}. It should affect how agents partition territory across zones and whether their risk profiles diverge, with those patterns becoming more or less stable as the environment grows noisier.

\subsection{Why the DQN family is the right substrate}
\label{sec:dqn-foundations}

The DQN family suits this comparison for several connected reasons. The decision space in this simulator is discrete. At each zone, an agent holds or attempts an overtake at one of three risk levels. Q-value methods maintain separate estimates for each action, which fits this structure \citep{zhu2021actionspaces,mnih2015human}.

Experience replay also matters in a five-lap race with sparse decisions. Value-based methods can reuse past transitions, learning from rare but informative events more efficiently than on-policy methods that discard experience after a single update \citep{mnih2015human,schaul2016prioritized}.

The Q-value overestimation problem is directly relevant. Vanilla Q-learning inflates action values in stochastic environments because the Bellman max operator is biased upward under variance. Double DQN corrects this by separating action selection from value evaluation in the target \citep{vanhasselt2016double}. Noisy overtake outcomes inflate Q-values for aggressive actions, biasing agents toward overconfident risk-taking in stochastic conditions.

The dueling architecture reduces noise in states where holding is almost always correct. Separating state value from action advantage \citep{wang2016dueling} prevents the advantage head from noisily differentiating actions that have near-identical expected outcomes.

Comparing variants within the same family under identical infrastructure isolates the effect of each mechanism. Differences in emergent strategy across alpha values must be attributable to incentive structure, not to algorithm-level differences \citep{henderson2018deep,agarwal2021statistical}. DQN-family independent learners are the standard first baseline in the MARL literature for value-based settings \citep{hernandezleal2019survey}. Identifying which variant is resistant to collapse in the competitive baseline is therefore a prerequisite for the incentive-regime comparison.

\section{The research gap and this project's contribution}

No existing motorsport study has varied the incentive structure between concurrently learning agents in a stochastic simulator, and no existing MARL study has measured whether such variation produces genuine cooperative advantage or merely redistributes competitive outcomes. Neither question has been combined with a matched-budget evaluation protocol reporting confidence intervals and behavioural diagnostics alongside raw win rate.

This project addresses that gap. Race strategy is framed as a social dilemma where incentive structure, rather than algorithm choice, determines emergent behaviour. A reward sharing coefficient spanning purely competitive to fully cooperative incentives is implemented and its effect measured across both zero-sum and non-zero-sum game structures. A controlled ablation across agent counts identifies a sharp scaling boundary at which cooperative incentive transitions from beneficial to harmful. Curriculum alpha scheduling reveals that what prior literature treated as a single convergence failure is in fact two separate problems. One is caused by training order and resolves with curriculum. The other is a credit assignment problem that requires architectural solutions. A mathematical proof establishes that a mean-field approximation of difference rewards \citep{wolpert2002optimal} inverts the cooperative gradient. A zero-shot large language model provides a non-learning comparator.

The testbed is an F1-inspired simulator set at the Spa-Francorchamps circuit, with nine overtaking zones derived from historical telemetry and a configurable reward contract. Aerodynamic fidelity and tyre degradation are excluded to keep the dynamics under study interpretable. Three stochasticity levels test whether findings hold as the environment becomes less predictable. Full definitions are in Chapter~\ref{chapter2}.

\section{Aim, objectives, and research questions}

\subsection{Aim}

This project uses a stochastic F1-inspired racing simulator to study how sharing reward between competing learning agents changes the strategies they develop. A single parameter controls how much each agent values a teammate's finishing position alongside its own, and varying this across competitive and cooperative game settings is the primary experiment. Drawing on both reinforcement learning and game theory, the investigation examines how agents behave under purely individual incentives, whether partial reward sharing produces genuine coordination or causes learning to collapse, and how these effects shift as more agents are introduced.

\subsection{Objectives}

The project has four objectives. The first is to design and validate a stochastic racing simulator with configurable complexity, reproducible seeding, and structured behavioural telemetry. The second is to identify which DQN-family variant provides a stable learning substrate for the incentive regime comparison. The third is to characterise how varying the reward sharing coefficient affects emergent strategy, learning stability, and cooperative performance across multiple stochasticity levels and agent counts. The fourth is to evaluate difference rewards as an alternative cooperative formulation and establish whether a mean-field approximation preserves the cooperative gradient property \citep{wolpert2002optimal}.

\subsection{Research questions and hypotheses}

\textbf{RQ1.} What patterns of strategic behaviour emerge when independent reinforcement learning agents train concurrently in a shared stochastic environment, and how sensitive are those patterns to environmental noise?

\textbf{H1.} Concurrent training is predicted to produce interpretable strategic differentiation, including asymmetric risk preferences and differentiated zone usage, as agents adapt to each other's evolving policies through iterated best-response dynamics. Higher environmental stochasticity is expected to flatten Q-value gradients across zones, reducing the reliability of any single zone's expected return and weakening the basis for stable specialisation.

\textbf{RQ2.} How does systematically varying the reward sharing coefficient between agents alter the emergent strategy profile, and is there a threshold beyond which shared incentive destabilises rather than improves learning?

\textbf{H2.} Increasing the reward sharing coefficient from zero is predicted to initially encourage strategic differentiation, as partial alignment creates mild pressure to avoid competing for the same zone. Beyond a critical threshold, the cooperative term is expected to dominate individual gradients, inverting the incentive to improve own position and producing convergence to passive equilibria. This transition is predicted to be sharp rather than gradual, based on the structural relationship between the cooperative reward term and the individual component. Whether the threshold shifts with agent count, and whether training schedule can recover lost performance, are sub-questions that follow naturally from establishing the threshold's existence.

\textbf{RQ3.} Does shared incentive produce genuine cooperative advantage, measured as improved joint performance against a common adversary, or does it merely redistribute outcomes within a fixed interaction?

\textbf{H3.} In a zero-sum interaction, shared incentive cannot produce genuine cooperative advantage because joint outcomes are structurally fixed. Introducing a common adversary breaks this constraint. Under non-zero-sum conditions, partial incentive alignment is predicted to increase joint performance by encouraging zone differentiation and reducing competitive waste. Fully cooperative incentive may restore pathological convergence if the individual gradient becomes too weak to sustain coherent strategy.
