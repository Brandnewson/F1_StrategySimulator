\chapter{Results, Evaluation and Discussion}
\label{chapter4}

This chapter reports the empirical findings of Phases~2 to~10 organised around the four research questions from Chapter~\ref{chapter1}. Phase~9 is reported separately as a validity check on the investigation.

\section{Preliminary validation}
\label{sec:prelim}

The three preliminary gates all passed. Phase~0 system integrity passed all 12 tests after 2 defects were corrected (Appendix~\ref{appendix:phase01}). Phase~1 smoke validation cleared the 0.50 gate across all s0 seeds with CI lower bound also clearing 0.50. Phase~2 established the DQN-family single-agent ranking at s0 as rainbow-lite (0.835, CI95 0.813-0.856), double (0.798), vanilla (0.746, CI95 0.723-0.769), and dueling (0.724), with rainbow-lite's interval not overlapping vanilla.

Rainbow-lite's s0 to s2 change is $+0.005$, consistent with PER and double-target correction reducing value instability \citep{schaul2016prioritized,vanhasselt2016double}. Dueling ranked below vanilla, suggesting value-advantage decomposition did not help in this two-agent setting. Rainbow-lite is the leading single-agent substrate candidate. Phase~2's single-agent contract cannot establish MARL viability on its own. Whether the ranking transfers to concurrent learning is addressed in Section~\ref{sec:rq1}, where the Phase~3 collapse ablation both answers RQ1 and confirms the substrate choice.

\section{RQ1: What strategic behaviours emerge and how robust are they?}
\label{sec:rq1}

Phase~3 tested four DQN-family variants under concurrent learning at $\alpha = 0$, where two instances of the same variant race each other in a zero-sum setting. Each variant probes a different hypothesis about what drives MARL pathologies in this setting. Vanilla DQN is the control. Double DQN isolates whether the problem is maximum-operator overestimation, where rare lucky successes at hard zones inflate Q-values for bad actions. Dueling DQN isolates whether the problem is representational, where separating state-value from action-advantage might stabilise learning in states where action choice matters less. Rainbow-lite tests the combined substrate hypothesis, where multiple coupled issues (overestimation, sampling imbalance, credit propagation) need addressing together. Collapse rates across nine trials per algorithm are in Table~\ref{tab:phase3-collapse}.

\centering

\label{tab:phase3-collapse}
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|c|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Algorithm} & \textbf{s0} & \textbf{s1} & \textbf{s2} & \textbf{Total collapse} \\
\hline
Vanilla DQN   & 1/3 & 1/3 & 2/3 & 4/9 (44\%) \\
Double DQN    & 1/3 & 0/3 & 0/3 & 1/9 (11\%) \\
Dueling DQN   & 1/3 & 1/3 & 2/3 & 4/9 (44\%) \\
Rainbow-lite  & 0/3 & 0/3 & 0/3 & 0/9 (0\%)  \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The ablation picks out two distinct failure modes. Double DQN addresses the first and largest source of error. In this domain, early training produces many failed attempts for the trailing agent punctuated by occasional lucky successes at hard zones, which inflates Q-values for the chosen action under vanilla's target computation. Double target correction dampens this by decoupling action selection from evaluation, which removes a real first-order source of error that affects many runs. Double eliminates collapse entirely at s1 and s2, with only one collapse at s0.

Dueling DQN fails to help and has the same collapse rate as vanilla. This is not because dueling is a poor algorithm, but because its architectural advantage targets a problem this simulator does not really have. Dueling's decomposition of $Q(s,a)$ into state-value and action-advantage works best when many actions are near-equivalent in most states, so that separating "is this state good?" from "which action matters?" reduces learning variance. The overtaking decision is the opposite case. HOLD versus ATTEMPT, and risk level within ATTEMPT, are strongly differentiated in outcome. The action split is where most of the information sits. In this setting, dueling's decomposition can actually sharpen bad action preferences rather than smooth them, making the agent converge to a bad strategy faster once it drifts into one.

Rainbow-lite closes the remaining gap from 11 per cent collapse under double to zero. Prioritised experience replay (PER) is the load-bearing addition in this marginal improvement. Dueling is neutral or negative in this domain (Phase 3 ablation), and multi-step returns are inferentially secondary because they are not separately isolated in the ablation design. PER addresses the second failure mode: sampling imbalance in the replay buffer. After double has removed the broad overestimation problem, what remains is a harder residual where the buffer accumulates systematic bias from positional asymmetry. Early failed attempts by the trailing agent produce $\langle$attempt, negative$\rangle$ transitions that dominate uniform replay. $Q(\text{hold})$ then rises above $Q(\text{attempt})$, and shared epsilon decay locks in passivity before exploration can recover. PER breaks this lock-in by up-weighting high TD-error transitions, which are disproportionately the rare successful attempts that need to be learned from. This account of the collapse mechanism is grounded in the ablation logic and in the observed collapse pattern, where one agent gets all zone encounters and makes zero attempts. The replay buffer contents are not directly instrumented during training, so the account is inferred from the external signature rather than verified internally.

The ablation hierarchy therefore reflects two separable contributions: double target correction removes the large first-order overestimation error that drives most collapses, and PER closes the residual sampling trap that double cannot reach. To test whether collapse under vanilla was simply a case of the training horizon being too short, vanilla was extended to 750 episodes. Collapse consistently worsened across seeds rather than recovering, confirming that once the loop engages it is self-reinforcing within the training horizon \citep{foerster2017stabilising,matignon2012independent}. The Phase~3 ablation therefore confirms the Phase~2 selection under MARL conditions. Rainbow-lite is the only variant that survives concurrent learning and is used as the substrate for all later phases, with double DQN retained in the Phase~3 family ablation and vanilla in Phase~10 as a weak-algorithm comparator.

Where the algorithm prevents collapse, agents develop differentiated zone coverage and opposing risk profiles without any shared coordination. A representative rainbow-lite trial at s2 with 750 episodes reaches a zone differentiation index of 0.405: Agent~1 focuses on La Source, Les Combes, and Bruxelles with a conservative bias, Agent~2 covers five zones with an aggressive bias. The pattern echoes the hawk-dove game from evolutionary game theory \citep{maynardsmith1973hawkdove}, where two players contesting a shared resource settle into asymmetric roles (one aggressive, one passive) rather than both playing aggressively at the cost of mutual destruction. Here the equilibrium emerges through iterated best-response learning, where each agent gradually adjusts its policy to be optimal against the current policy of the other, converging to a stable joint profile \citep{busoniu2008survey}. Collapsed trials produce no such structure. One agent stops competing entirely and the other wins unopposed, matching the degenerate equilibrium predicted for non-cooperative two-agent Q-learning under positional disadvantage \citep{tan1993multiagent}.

Specialisation depends on training budget, though with high across-seed variance. Seed 202 rises from 0.188 at 500 episodes to 0.405 at 750 (the canonical case), seed 101 from 0.022 to 0.163, and seed 303 from 0.064 to 0.054 (no recovery). The two diagnostic seeds (404 and 505) sampled only at 500 episodes bracket a wide range (0.043 and 0.520), showing some initial conditions reach high differentiation early while others do not. The pattern is budget-sensitive rather than budget-determined. PER needs sufficient zone-specific transitions to produce reliable relative priorities, but initialisation variance also matters for how quickly this stabilises.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.88\textwidth]{diagrams/phase3_zone_specialization_growth.png}
\caption{Zone differentiation growth for rainbow-lite at s2 showing dependence on training budget.}
\label{fig:phase3-zone-specialisation-growth}
\end{figure}

A crossplay protocol at s2 revealed an 11 percentage point structural bias against the A1 evaluation slot, affecting raw A1 win rates but not collapse classification. Two diagnostic follow-up seeds (404 and 505) were run to test whether the original three-seed pattern reflected systematic policy asymmetry or an evaluation artefact. Seed 505 produced an A1 win rate of 0.560 at s2, breaking the apparent ceiling from the original three seeds. After applying the 11 percentage point bias correction, four of five rainbow-lite seeds sit at or above parity, and only seed 101 shows a genuine A2 edge. The apparent A2 dominance at s2 largely dissolves under correction, which supports the evaluation-bias interpretation rather than a systematic policy effect. Non-stationarity rather than stochasticity dominates Phase~3: vanilla and dueling collapse rises from 1/3 at s0 to 2/3 at s2, showing stochasticity accelerates but does not cause collapse \citep{foerster2017stabilising}.

\textbf{RQ1 answer.} H1 predicted differentiated zone coverage and opposing risk profiles through iterated best-response dynamics without explicit coordination. The evidence supports this, but the support is conditional on the algorithm. Vanilla and dueling DQN collapse in 44 per cent of trials and produce no strategic structure at all, so H1 applies only where the algorithm survives the non-stationarity challenge. In all nine rainbow-lite trials and in eight of nine double DQN trials, asymmetric strategic differentiation emerges without explicit coordination. Rainbow-lite produces hawk-dove risk partitioning as the clearest case but also shows other competitive equilibria. Double DQN's non-collapsed trials sometimes show zone-avoidance asymmetry or wrong-zone fixation rather than clean hawk-dove partitioning. H1 is confirmed for collapse-resistant algorithms, with two qualifications. Specialisation depends jointly on training budget and initialisation variance rather than budget alone, and non-stationarity from concurrent learning is the dominant challenge rather than environmental stochasticity.

\section{RQ2: How does incentive structure alter strategy?}
\label{sec:rq2}

Phase~4 ran the $\alpha$ sweep in the two-agent zero-sum game. Collapse is low at $\alpha \le 0.5$, sharp between $\alpha = 0.5$ and $\alpha = 0.75$, and complete at $\alpha = 1.0$ (Table~\ref{tab:phase4-collapse}). At $\alpha \ge 0.75$ the cooperative reward term dominates the individual gradient, turning successful overtakes into net penalties and driving both agents to passivity \citep{claus1998dynamics}. The exact 8/9 figure at $\alpha = 0.75$ is numerically imprecise under the three-seed design, though the qualitative pattern is robust.

Two further patterns within Phase~4 clarify the nature of the threshold. Zone convention formation is not monotonic in $\alpha$ (it does not rise steadily from low to high). Non-degenerate zone differentiation peaks at $\alpha = 0.25$ and declines thereafter. Partial alignment initially promotes differentiation as agents coordinate on complementary zones, but stronger alignment then erases that convention as both agents converge to a risk-averse policy. Convention formation and threshold stability are not aligned along $\alpha$.

The role of stochasticity also inverts across the threshold. At $\alpha \le 0.5$ noise is stabilising, breaking potential lock-in patterns before they consolidate. Noise also weakens zone conventions themselves, so the alpha = 0.25 conventions attenuate sharply under increasing noise. Together these patterns show $\alpha$ changing the type of equilibrium, not just the strength of a single one. Low $\alpha$ produces competitive convention with differentiated zone coverage. High $\alpha$ produces an unstable passive equilibrium where both agents learn to hold.

Phase~5 ran the same sweep in the minimal non-zero-sum configuration (two DQN agents plus one Base adversary). The curve is fundamentally different (Table~\ref{tab:phase5-results}). Collapse disappears at all $\alpha$ values. Joint beat-base rate follows an inverted-U peaking at $\alpha = 0.75$ with an 83 per cent improvement over the $\alpha = 0$ baseline. There is no destabilisation threshold here, only a performance optimum. Full cooperation at $\alpha = 1.0$ stays active but becomes higher-variance and less consistent than $\alpha = 0.75$, which sharpens the interpretation that 0.75 is a genuine optimum rather than a monotone rising region that saturates.

Cooperation has a minimum effective dose. At $\alpha = 0.25$ joint beat-base drops 5 per cent below the $\alpha = 0$ baseline, so weak sharing is worse than none. Meaningful benefit starts at $\alpha = 0.5$ and peaks at $\alpha = 0.75$. Small cooperative signals plausibly introduce noise into the individual gradient without inducing role partitioning, leaving agents with weakened incentive signal and no coordination benefit. Above $\alpha = 0.5$ the signal is strong enough to drive complementary role development.

The mechanism behind the cooperative gain is redistributive rather than uniform. Agent~2 rises from 63 per cent beat-base at $\alpha = 0$ to 78 per cent at $\alpha = 0.75$, while Agent~1's rate changes less. The joint metric moves because the floor rises. This is consistent with cooperative incentive functioning as a mechanism that closes within-team performance gaps rather than lifting both agents in parallel \citep{hughes2018inequity}. Strategy and performance metrics also separate. Zone differentiation peaks at low $\alpha$, risk differentiation increases with $\alpha$, and joint performance peaks at $\alpha = 0.75$. More differentiation does not produce better performance on its own.

\textbf{RQ2 answer.} H2 predicted a sharp threshold above which shared reward destabilises learning, with non-zero-sum structure reversing the effect. The zero-sum part is confirmed at the $\alpha = 0.5$ to $\alpha = 0.75$ boundary, with a non-monotonic convention-formation pattern below and a stochasticity inversion across it. The non-zero-sum part does not simply reverse. The destabilisation disappears, replaced by an inverted-U performance curve with a minimum effective dose somewhere between $\alpha = 0.25$ (harmful) and $\alpha = 0.5$ (helpful) and an optimum at $\alpha = 0.75$. The gain comes primarily from lifting the weaker agent rather than from uniform improvement. H2 is therefore confirmed in its zero-sum part and restated in its non-zero-sum part. $\alpha$ changes the type of equilibrium rather than only the direction of its effect. Phase~5 establishes that cooperative advantage exists in the minimal non-zero-sum setting and is non-monotonic in $\alpha$. Whether that advantage survives at larger agent counts is addressed in RQ3.

\section{RQ3: Does shared incentive produce genuine cooperative advantage at scale?}
\label{sec:rq3}

RQ2 established that cooperative advantage exists in the minimal non-zero-sum setting and produces a sharp performance optimum at $\alpha = 0.75$. RQ3 tests whether this survives scaling, and the failure mode that emerges is importantly different from anything in RQ2. Across Phases 6, 7, and 8, collapse remains at zero in all 72 trials. The system stays active at scale. What disappears is the cooperative surplus. Agents still race and still respond to $\alpha$, but shared reward stops producing within-team co-improvement.

Phase~6 scaled to four DQN agents in two teams of two plus the Base adversary. Every tested form of cooperation degrades performance. At symmetric $\alpha = 0.75$, joint beat-base drops 31 per cent below the competitive baseline. In the asymmetric condition the cooperative team does not just underperform against the baseline, it loses directly to the competitive team in head-to-head comparison. Team A at $\alpha = 0.75$ wins only 47.5 per cent of races against Team B at $\alpha = 0.0$, with worse average position and lower both-beat-base (Table~\ref{tab:phase6-results}). with worse average position and lower both-beat-base (Table~\ref{tab:phase6-results}). The Base adversary benefits directly from cooperative-team degradation. Under symmetric $\alpha = 0.75$, Base moves from near last in the competitive baseline to near third in the field ranking. Curriculum scheduling largely restores Base to its original position, confirming that the cooperative-team caution under fixed alpha opens space the Base heuristic can exploit.

The strongest mechanistic evidence is the persistently negative intra-team position-delta correlation. It is negative in all 36 Phase~6 trials. Shared reward between teammates never becomes genuine within-team co-improvement at this scale, regardless of $\alpha$ or condition. The root cause is causal dilution of the pairwise cooperative signal. Reward sharing remains within teammate pairs, but at four agents each teammate's outcome is now influenced by three competitors rather than one. The pairwise signal carries weaker causal information about the teammate's actions, so updates become noisier and agents learn caution rather than coordination \citep{agogino2004unifying}.

The failure is not an observation-space problem. The observation contract included teammate-aware features (teammate-ahead and teammate-behind indicators) that agents could have conditioned on, but no team-conditional behaviour emerges. Agents have the information needed for coordination but lack the reward signal to learn from it. The bottleneck is credit assignment rather than missing teammate information.

Phase~7 characterised the scaling boundary. The $\alpha = 0.75$ effect moves from $+83\%$ at three agents, to $+5\%$ at four agents (Phase~7A, within noise), to $-31\%$ at five agents (Phase~6), shown in Figure~\ref{fig:phase5to7-cooperative-cliff}. Part of the drop from 0.310 to 0.159 reflects a baseline compounding effect where a fourth agent requires one more DQN to beat Base, pushing the joint rate from $p^2$ to $p^3$ territory. The meaningful result is that $\alpha = 0.75$ fails to create any synergy above this compounded baseline. Individual beat-base rates also barely move under $\alpha$ in Phase~7A, staying roughly in a 52 to 61 per cent band while joint beat-base sits at the independence baseline. $\alpha$ changes structure without producing synergy.

Phase~7B tested whether any $\alpha$ value recovers cooperative performance at five agents. Combined with Phase~6 reference points at $\alpha = 0.0$, $0.5$, and $0.75$, the full five-agent team-$\alpha$ sweep is essentially monotonic downward (0.350, 0.344, 0.349, 0.315, 0.306, 0.243 at $\alpha = 0.00, 0.10, 0.15, 0.25, 0.50, 0.75$). No $\alpha$ value recovers performance, and the failure is structural rather than parametric. Zone specialisation is necessary but not sufficient for cooperative advantage. At four agents, $\alpha = 0.75$ raised zone differentiation by 63 per cent relative to $\alpha = 0$, yet produced no joint benefit. Attempt rates simultaneously dropped 38 to 64 per cent across zones, so agents responded to the cooperative signal by becoming more cautious rather than coordinating effectively.

Phase~8 tested whether curriculum scheduling recovers cooperative performance. At five agents (Phase~8B) curriculum recovered 69 per cent of the performance loss (joint beat-base rising from 0.243 to 0.318 against the 0.350 baseline), and excessive caution fell 86 to 89 per cent. At four agents (Phase~8A) curriculum almost eliminated the caution pathology. Curriculum therefore solves the training-order damage that high fixed $\alpha$ causes. What curriculum does not produce is cooperation. In no condition does curriculum exceed the competitive baseline. The intra-team correlation becomes even more negative under curriculum (-0.189 in Phase~8B), meaning agents improve individually while actively anti-coordinating. Curriculum cleanly separates two failure modes often discussed together in earlier literature \citep{panait2005cooperative, zhao2025maca}. It resolves training-order collapse. It does not resolve credit-assignment failure. At four or more agents, reward-level interventions appear to be insufficient, which points toward architectural changes such as CTDE-style methods as the next plausible route \citep{rashid2018qmix}.

\textbf{RQ3 answer.} H3 predicted that beyond a certain agent count, no $\alpha$ value and no training schedule would recover cooperation because the problem is credit assignment rather than training order. Phase~7B's monotonic downward curve and Phase~8's recovered-but-anti-coordinating outcome both confirm this, and the persistently negative intra-team correlation across all scaling phases is the direct evidence. The scaling failure is not passive collapse but active anti-coordination. Agents remain active, $\alpha$ still changes zone allocation, and curriculum restores aggressive play, but none of these changes produce positive within-team coupling. H3 is confirmed. The curriculum clause of H2 finds support in Phase~8's 69 per cent recovery, which is real but insufficient for cooperation. The structural boundary sits at four total agents in this simulator.

\section{RQ4: Do alternative cooperative reward formulations preserve the advantage?}
\label{sec:rq4}

Phase~10 tested the mean-field difference reward approximation (Section~\ref{sec:difference-rewards}) at the four-total-agent configuration (3 DQN + Base), matching the Phase~7A setup where alpha-mixed cooperation had already failed to produce advantage. The test is therefore whether an alternative cooperative formulation recovers advantage at the scaling boundary where alpha mixing broke down, not whether it preserves the Phase~5 three-agent advantage.

The implemented formula has two structural properties that together explain why it cannot produce cooperation. First, the total reward mass is conserved because the sum of teammate rewards equals the sum of their individual deltas, so the formula redistributes credit rather than adding cooperative mass. Second, differentiating any agent's reward with respect to a teammate's delta gives $-1/N$, which is negative. Differentiating with respect to that same agent's own delta gives $(2N-1)/N$, which is amplified relative to the identity. The approximation therefore amplifies own-performance incentive and penalises teammate improvement.

The empirical result matches the mechanism. Difference rewards rank last among the cooperative formulations tested at the four-agent boundary. The original Phase~10 run used vanilla DQN by mistake, producing 0.087 joint beat-base. The corrected rainbow-lite rerun rose to 0.133 but still sits below the 0.159 IQL baseline, confirming the negative result survives removal of the algorithm confound. The 31 per cent relative algorithm effect (vanilla to rainbow-lite under difference rewards) exceeds the 16 per cent relative formula effect (IQL to rainbow-lite difference rewards), so algorithm choice remains determining even under a changed reward formula.

The deficit is not primarily a failure against the Base adversary. Base average position in the corrected rainbow-lite run sits at 2.72, comparable to Phase~7A IQL's 2.69. The joint shortfall reflects the DQN group failing to convert individual competitiveness into joint beat-base success rather than failing to push Base down the field. This is consistent with the structural property analysis. Reward amplification for own-performance keeps individual agents active, but the negative teammate gradient prevents the joint metric from rising.

\textbf{RQ4 answer.} H4 predicted the approximation would invert the cooperative signal and fail to recover cooperative advantage at the four-agent boundary. Confirmed. The implemented mean-field difference reward redistributes credit rather than adding cooperative mass, amplifies own-performance incentive, and penalises teammate improvement. It therefore underperforms all tested four-agent baselines. The result is about this specific approximation, not about Wolpert-Tumer difference rewards in general. A formulation that preserves the counterfactual property of the original literature may or may not produce cooperation at this scale, which is a question for future work. Within the scope of RQ4's test, cooperative advantage requires a reward formula with positive cooperative gradients.

\section{Validity check: LLM semantic baseline}
\label{sec:llm-validity}

Phase~9 (Section~\ref{sec:llm-baseline}) compared trained rainbow-lite against a zero-shot LLM with hand-authored strategic priors, across five seeds with 150 races per seed. Rainbow-lite achieved 0.835, 0.839, and 0.840 at s0, s1, s2 respectively, against the LLM's 0.819, 0.827, and 0.837. The gap is not statistically meaningful. Strategies differed qualitatively. The LLM selected NORMAL at La Source, reserved CONSERVATIVE for difficulty-0.7 zones, avoided Raidillon entirely, and used Stavelot in a pattern rainbow-lite never developed. The parity suggests the simulator's narrow strategy space admits a short near-optimal policy captured by hand-authored priors \citep{nagpal2025llm_mca}. This qualifies the scope of the MARL findings rather than invalidating them, and identifies specific RL-specific pathologies (Raidillon overuse, suboptimal Stavelot behaviour) that domain reasoning avoids. Whether the same dynamics hold in richer environments is a question for future work.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Synthesis}

The four RQs together characterise cooperative learning in this simulator. Algorithm choice determines whether competitive equilibrium is maintained (RQ1). Reward sharing produces a sharp threshold whose direction reverses with game structure (RQ2). Cooperative advantage scales only to three agents and cannot be recovered at four or more by reward-level interventions (RQ3). The mean-field difference rewards approximation fails to recover cooperative advantage at the four-agent boundary because it redistributes credit without adding cooperative mass (RQ4). Where cooperation emerges, three structural conditions must hold: non-zero-sum game structure, a collapse-resistant algorithm, and a reward formula with positive cooperative gradients. Where any one fails, the system degrades in the characteristic mode associated with that failure.

\subsection{Validity threats and limitations}
\label{sec:validity}

\textbf{Simulator scope.} La Source has base success probability 0.8, so early-phase win rates partly reflect exploitation of one dominant zone. The five-lap Spa format produces sparse decisions and may not generalise to circuits with more balanced zone difficulty.

\textbf{Evaluation limitations.} Crossplay reveals an 11 percentage point structural bias against the A1 slot affecting raw Phase~3 win rates (though not collapse classification). Three seeds per cell widens confidence intervals, though it does not affect the non-overlapping Phase~2 s0 ranking.

\textbf{Hyperparameter tuning scope.} Hyperparameters were tuned against the Phase~2 single-agent contract, so the configuration reflects single-agent stability rather than multi-agent dynamics. Different configurations may produce different Phase~3 onwards results.

\textbf{LLM prompt confound.} The Phase~9 LLM used a hand-authored prompt with strategic priors, so parity measures the language model and prompt jointly. A raw-state prompt would be a stronger comparison.

\textbf{Base Agent as comparator.} Beat-base rates are relative to a specific heuristic and would shift under a stronger or weaker comparator, though they remain internally consistent across phases.

\subsection{Future directions}
\label{sec:futurework}

\textbf{Technical improvements.} QMIX addresses the credit assignment barrier at four or more agents with a mixing network preserving cooperative gradients \citep{rashid2018qmix,amato2024ctde_intro}. A potential-based formulation of difference rewards \citep{devlin2014potential} would test whether the Phase~10 failure is specific to the mean-field approximation or to the concept more broadly.

\textbf{Direct extensions.} Medium-complexity simulators with tyre degradation and pit-stops would test whether LLM parity holds in richer state spaces. Rerunning Phase~7 and~8 with a stronger comparator would test whether the scaling cliff's location is an artefact of comparator strength.

\textbf{Wider impact.} The three structural conditions for cooperative advantage (non-zero-sum game structure, a collapse-resistant algorithm, and a reward formula with positive cooperative gradients) apply to any mixed-motive setting with self-interested agents sharing a common goal. The phased validation methodology is portable across MARL investigations for separating methodological failures from scientific ones.
