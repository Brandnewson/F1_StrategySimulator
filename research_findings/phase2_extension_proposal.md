# Phase 2 Extension Proposal — Algorithm Scope Review
**Date:** 2026-03-26
**Purpose:** Senior ML research assessment of whether to introduce a fifth algorithm into the benchmark, and which candidates are most justified given the research aim.

---

## 1. The Scope Question

The current Phase 2 dataset uses four DQN-family algorithms, of which two (vanilla, rainbow-lite) are carried forward to MARL phases. The concern is whether two algorithms is sufficient to make the incentive-mapping findings generalisable, or whether the results might be dismissed as DQN-family artefacts.

This is a legitimate concern. If vanilla and rainbow-lite both identify α* ≈ 0.5 as the cooperation threshold in Phase 4, a reader could reasonably ask: is that threshold a property of the incentive structure, or a property of value-based Q-learning in discrete action spaces? Adding one well-chosen algorithm from a different class would allow that question to be answered.

However, the wrong response is to add an algorithm to Phase 2 for the sake of breadth. Every additional algorithm must earn its place by testing a specific claim the current set cannot test.

## 3. Candidate Algorithms

### 3.1 Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO)

**Paper:** Schulman, J., Wolski, F., Dhariwal, P., Radford, A., & Klimov, O. (2017). *Proximal Policy Optimization Algorithms.* arXiv:1707.06347
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1707.06347

PPO is the dominant policy-gradient algorithm in applied RL research and the most widely deployed MARL baseline. It uses a clipped surrogate objective to constrain policy update size, making it far more stable than earlier policy gradient methods (REINFORCE, A3C) without the complexity of trust-region methods (TRPO).

**Mechanism:** PPO is an actor-critic method. A shared network outputs both a policy π(a|s) (the actor) and a state value V(s) (the critic). The actor is updated to increase the probability of actions that outperformed the critic's estimate (positive advantage), with a clip that prevents the update from changing the policy too drastically in a single step.

**Pros for this research:**
- On-policy training means PPO always learns from data generated by its current policy. When the opponent changes (MARL non-stationarity), PPO adapts faster than off-policy methods because it never replays stale transitions from an earlier opponent policy.
- The stochastic policy π(a|s) naturally produces mixed strategies — PPO can maintain a 60% conservative / 40% aggressive distribution as a genuine probability, whereas DQN's ε-greedy is a forced randomisation, not a learned distribution.
- Under the α sweep, a stochastic policy can express partial cooperation (e.g., cooperating with probability proportional to α) more naturally than a deterministic greedy policy.
- MAPPO (Multi-Agent PPO) extends directly to the Phase 4 team setting with minimal additional engineering.

**Cons:**
- Sample inefficiency: on-policy methods require fresh rollouts after every gradient update. In this simulator, each rollout is one race (5 laps). Convergence typically requires 3–5× more race simulations than DQN-family for equivalent policy quality.
- The clipped objective requires tuning (clip ratio ε, entropy coefficient, GAE λ). With wrong hyperparameters, PPO can collapse or oscillate.
- Comparability concern: mixing on-policy (PPO) and off-policy (DQN) results in the same table is methodologically awkward. Differences in performance could be attributed to training regime rather than algorithm design, which muddies the Phase 2 message.

**Verdict for Phase 2 extension:** Justified if framed as an algorithm-class comparison (value-based vs policy-gradient), but requires explicit acknowledgement that the comparison is confounded by on-policy vs off-policy sampling. A cleaner placement is as the MARL-phase comparator rather than a Phase 2 extension.

### 3.2 Soft Actor-Critic (SAC)

**Paper:** Haarnoja, T., Zhou, A., Abbeel, P., & Levine, S. (2018). *Soft Actor-Critic: Off-Policy Maximum Entropy Deep Reinforcement Learning with a Stochastic Actor.* arXiv:1801.01290
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1801.01290

SAC combines off-policy learning (replay buffer, like DQN) with a stochastic policy optimised to maximise both expected return and policy entropy. The entropy term acts as a built-in exploration mechanism and prevents premature convergence to a deterministic policy.

**Pros for this research:**
- Off-policy: sample efficiency is closer to DQN-family than PPO. Comparisons in the Phase 2 table are less confounded.
- Entropy maximisation directly addresses the bimodal collapse observed in dueling DQN at s0. An entropy-regularised agent cannot collapse to a policy with zero probability on any action — NORMAL risk would never be suppressed to 0%.
- The temperature parameter α (note: different from the team incentive α in Phase 4) provides direct control over exploration vs exploitation, which could be tuned to match DQN's ε schedule at a fair comparison budget.
- Discrete SAC variants exist (Christodoulou, 2019: arXiv:1910.07207) that directly apply to this discrete-action setting without continuous-action approximations.

**Cons:**
- Originally designed for continuous action spaces; discrete SAC is a more recent adaptation with less empirical validation.
- Adds a second α parameter to the experiment (entropy temperature), which clashes notionally with the team incentive α in Phase 4. Would require consistent relabelling.
- Less established as a MARL baseline compared to PPO; most MARL benchmarks use IL-PPO or QMIX, not IL-SAC.

**Verdict for Phase 2 extension:** Theoretically well-motivated — entropy regularisation directly addresses the dueling collapse finding. However, the discrete SAC implementation is more complex than discrete PPO, and the comparability with DQN-family at matched budgets requires careful justification. Not the first choice.

### 3.3 QMIX

**Paper:** Rashid, T., Samvelyan, M., de Witt, C.S., Farquhar, G., Foerster, J., & Whiteson, S. (2018). *QMIX: Monotonic Value Function Factorisation for Deep Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning.* Proceedings of ICML 2018. arXiv:1803.11605
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.11605

QMIX is a centralised-training / decentralised-execution (CTDE) method designed specifically for cooperative MARL. During training, a mixing network combines each agent's local Q-values into a joint team Q-value using a monotonic function. During execution, each agent uses only its own local Q-network. The monotonicity constraint ensures that the individual agents' greedy actions are also jointly optimal for the team.

**Pros for this research:**
- Directly encodes team-level reward during training — exactly what the Phase 4 cooperative setting (α=1) requires. QMIX is the canonical CTDE baseline for cooperative tasks.
- The mixing network's input can include the team incentive parameter α, making it possible to train a single QMIX network that conditions on α rather than training separate networks for each α level.
- Comparing IL-MARL (vanilla/rainbow acting independently) against CTDE (QMIX with global state) at the same α level directly tests whether centralised training changes the cooperation threshold α*. This is a more interesting research question than comparing two IL-MARL algorithms.
- Strong prior literature baseline: QMIX has been benchmarked on StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC) and numerous cooperative games. Results here would be contextualised against a rich body of prior work.

**Cons:**
- QMIX requires a global state during training — the mixing network must observe all agents' states simultaneously. The current simulator does not expose a global state tensor; this requires implementation work.
- QMIX is designed for fully cooperative settings. In Phase 4, the competitive between-team dynamics sit outside QMIX's monotonicity assumption — you would need one QMIX per team, not one across all four agents.
- Adds significant implementation complexity (mixing network, global state aggregation, hyperparameter sets for PER within QMIX).
- Placing QMIX in Phase 2 (single-agent) does not make sense — QMIX requires multiple agents by design. It belongs in Phase 4.

**Verdict for Phase 2 extension:** Wrong phase. QMIX is not a single-agent algorithm. It should be considered as a Phase 4 CTDE extension, not a Phase 2 comparator.

### 3.4 MAPPO (Multi-Agent PPO)

**Paper:** Yu, C., Velu, A., Vinitsky, E., Wang, Y., Bayen, A., & Wu, Y. (2021). *The Surprising Effectiveness of MAPPO in Cooperative Multi-Agent Games.* NeurIPS 2021. arXiv:2103.01955
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.01955

MAPPO applies PPO in a multi-agent setting where each agent has a local actor but shares a centralised critic that conditions on all agents' observations. The central finding of the Yu et al. paper is that a well-tuned MAPPO consistently matches or outperforms more complex CTDE methods (QMIX, MADDPG) on cooperative benchmarks, with far less implementation complexity.

**Pros for this research:**
- The "surprisingly effective" result from Yu et al. (2021) is directly relevant: if MAPPO performs competitively with QMIX in cooperative benchmarks, it is a strong candidate for Phase 4's team extension without the global state engineering overhead.
- Centralised critic naturally incorporates teammate information during training, which is exactly what the α>0 cooperative setting requires — the critic can learn that what the teammate does affects the team's joint return.
- The paper explicitly studies mixed cooperative-competitive settings, which is precisely the Phase 4 game structure.
- Does not require monotonicity constraint (unlike QMIX), so it can handle the between-team competitive dynamics without per-team decomposition.

**Cons:**
- Still an on-policy method; sample efficiency concern applies. Would require more training episodes than DQN-family at a matched compute budget.
- Phase 2 extension does not apply — MAPPO requires multiple agents. Single-agent MAPPO degenerates to standard PPO.
- Implementation requires policy network, centralised value network, and PPO update logic — three distinct components vs DQN's one.

**Verdict for Phase 2 extension:** Not applicable in Phase 2. Strong candidate for Phase 4 CTDE comparison against IL-MARL (vanilla/rainbow).

---

### 3.5 MADDPG (Multi-Agent Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient)

**Paper:** Lowe, R., Wu, Y., Tamar, A., Harb, J., Abbeel, P., & Mordatch, I. (2017). *Multi-Agent Actor-Critic for Mixed Cooperative-Competitive Environments.* NeurIPS 2017. arXiv:1706.02275
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.02275

MADDPG extends DDPG (deterministic policy gradient) to multi-agent settings with centralised critics. It is specifically designed for mixed cooperative-competitive environments — which is exactly the Phase 4 game structure (within-team cooperative, between-team competitive). It is already stubbed in the codebase (currently falls back to BaseAgent with a warning).

**Pros for this research:**
- The only algorithm in this list explicitly designed for mixed cooperative-competitive games — an exact match for Phase 4.
- The centralised critic observes all agents' policies and states, making it well-suited to the Phase 4 team structure where both teammate and opponent behaviour affect individual returns.
- Already scaffolded in the codebase — implementation would complete an existing stub rather than building from scratch.
- The original MADDPG paper uses a particle-physics cooperative-competitive environment that is structurally similar to this research's 4-agent team setting.

**Cons:**
- Designed for continuous action spaces. Discretising the action space for MADDPG requires a Gumbel-softmax approximation or a discrete MADDPG variant, adding implementation complexity.
- DDPG is known to be training-unstable; MADDPG inherits this sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- Has been superseded by MAPPO in most cooperative benchmarks (Yu et al., 2021 demonstrated this empirically). Starting with MADDPG would be building on a methodology that more recent literature has moved beyond.

**Verdict for Phase 2 extension:** Not applicable in Phase 2. Less preferred than MAPPO for Phase 4 due to continuous-action design and training instability, despite existing codebase scaffolding.

## 4. The Algorithm Most Justified for Phase 2 Extension: PPO

If one algorithm is to be added to the Phase 2 single-agent benchmark, the strongest justification is for **independent PPO** — not because it is expected to outperform rainbow-lite, but because it tests a specific claim that the current DQN family cannot: **whether the behavioural findings (risk distribution, zone selection, noise robustness) are specific to value-based Q-learning or are reproducible by a policy-gradient method.**

### What PPO extension would test

A single-agent PPO trained against the Base Agent under the same protocol (500 episodes, 150 eval races, 3 seeds, s0/s1/s2) would allow the following comparison:

**Does the AGGRESSIVE flip under noise appear in PPO?** If yes, the overestimation mechanism cannot be the cause — PPO does not use Q-value targets at all. The flip would instead be attributable to reward signal instability (noisy rewards causing high-variance policy gradient estimates), which is a different mechanism with different implications for Phase 4.

**Does PPO develop a similarly conservative strategy to rainbow-lite?** Rainbow-lite's conservatism emerged from PER + n-step returns. If PPO also converges to a conservative zone-selection strategy without any of those mechanisms, that convergence is driven by the reward structure (low conservative failure cost) rather than algorithm design — a structurally important finding.

**Is PPO's zone discrimination better or worse than DQN's?** Policy gradient methods learn a stochastic policy that naturally assigns non-zero probability to all actions. This prevents the bimodal collapse seen in dueling. If PPO shows cleaner zone discrimination (HOLD at Raidillon, attempt at La Source) with a smoother risk distribution, it argues that the discrete Q-network architecture is part of the reason for dueling's failure — not just the 6-feature state space.

### The honest case against PPO in Phase 2

The comparison is confounded by on-policy vs off-policy sampling. DQN with 500 training episodes and a 150,000-transition replay buffer has access to a much richer training distribution than PPO with 500 episodes of fresh rollouts only. A fair comparison would require matching either:
- Training steps (not episode count), or
- Computational budget (wall-clock time or total race simulations)

Neither is straightforward. If PPO underperforms at matched episode count, the finding is ambiguous: weaker algorithm, or insufficient sample budget? If it performs equivalently, the finding is stronger. If it outperforms, the DQN-family comparison looks less robust.

The safest framing is: **PPO as a robustness check rather than a primary comparator.** Present it alongside the DQN results with the sampling confound explicitly acknowledged, not as a head-to-head ranking.

---

## 5. Recommendation

### Do not extend Phase 2 with another algorithm. Extend Phase 4 instead.

The research aim is about **incentive-to-behaviour mapping** — finding α* and testing whether pace asymmetry shifts it. Adding a fifth algorithm to Phase 2 dilutes this focus without directly answering the primary research question.

The more scientifically interesting extension is to include one non-DQN algorithm in **Phase 4** only, where the question becomes: *does the cooperation threshold α\* depend on algorithm class?* If vanilla, rainbow-lite, and PPO all identify α\* ≈ 0.5 in the team environment, the finding is algorithm-independent and therefore more generalisable. If PPO identifies a different threshold, that gap is itself a finding about how policy representation affects cooperative strategy emergence.

**Recommended additions by phase:**

| Phase | Extension | Justification |
|:------|:----------|:--------------|
| Phase 2 | None | DQN-family is exhaustively sampled; scope is appropriate for the single-agent reliability question |
| Phase 3 (IL-MARL baseline) | Add IL-PPO as a third agent | Establishes whether DQN non-stationarity findings generalise to policy-gradient agents; low implementation cost |
| Phase 4 (team extension) | Add MAPPO as CTDE comparator | Tests whether centralised training shifts α\*; directly answers the algorithm-class generalisation question without the Phase 2 confound |

### If scope pressure requires an earlier algorithm addition

If the dissertation committee or examiner requires a broader algorithm comparison before Phase 4, the pragmatic path is to add **PPO to Phase 3** (not Phase 2). Phase 3 is where the MARL framing begins, and an on-policy method's faster adaptation to non-stationarity is directly relevant to Phase 3's research question. The implementation is lighter than MAPPO (no centralised critic required) and the Phase 3 data will still feed into the Phase 4 α sweep with PPO as the third algorithm.

### What this gives the dissertation

With vanilla + rainbow-lite + PPO across Phase 3 and Phase 4, the dissertation can make the following claim:

> *"The cooperation threshold α\* ≈ [x] is identified consistently across two qualitatively different learning paradigms — a value-based off-policy method (rainbow-lite) and a policy-gradient on-policy method (PPO) — suggesting the threshold is a structural property of the incentive geometry rather than an artefact of the learning algorithm."*

That is a substantially stronger claim than the same finding from vanilla + rainbow-lite alone. It directly addresses the scope concern without bloating Phase 2.
